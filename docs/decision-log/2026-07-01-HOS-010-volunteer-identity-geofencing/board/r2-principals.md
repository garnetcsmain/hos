# Principals Review: HOS-2026-010-R2 (defining sensitive operations)

## My Recommendation

**A short, content-BLIND scope, gated by one criterion — and today that scope is EMPTY.**

The right rule is not a per-action list. It is a single criterion:

> **A volunteer/reporter action warrants a step-up (device-PIN re-entry) only if it is IRREVERSIBLE or DESTRUCTIVE to an existing record — i.e. it destroys, overwrites, or silently alters information a coordinator or family already relies on. Creating a new record is never sensitive; it is the thing HOS exists to make frictionless.**

Apply that criterion to HOS's *actual, verified* action surface as of 2026-07-01 and the result is: **nothing qualifies today.** Both volunteer-facing writes (`POST /api/found`, `POST /api/missing`) are append-only creates. There is no edit endpoint, no retract endpoint, no delete endpoint, and no identity-switch primitive in the codebase (verified — see files list). So the honest output of this round is:

1. **Define the criterion now** (irreversible/destructive-to-existing-record → step-up; pure create → never).
2. **Populate the list to EMPTY today**, because no action in the real surface meets the criterion.
3. **Pre-commit the criterion** so that IF an edit/retract endpoint is ever built, step-up attaches to it *by construction at design time*, not as a bolt-on nobody remembers.

On the three starter candidates specifically:

- **Candidate 1 (found-report with `condition=deceased`): REJECT step-up.** Content-conditional step-up — a server-side inspection of `condition` that decides whether to demand re-auth — is the wrong mechanism on principle (Logic Check C1, C2), and even a content-*blind* step-up on the create call is wrong because a create is not destructive. HOS **already** handles `deceased` correctly and at the correct layer: `familyReach.ts` (`isSensitiveOutcome`, Board D4) routes it to a *human coordinator* with compassionate-contact obligations, and the case resolves only when a human records the family was reached. That is where "sensitive" belongs — in the human obligation downstream, not in re-authenticating the reporter upstream.
- **Candidate 2 (edit/retract): the ONLY real member of the scope — but it does not exist.** IF built, it is exactly what the criterion catches (destructive to an existing record). Correct disposition: don't add friction to a phantom; add the *rule* now so the friction is born with the endpoint.
- **Candidate 3 (switch active volunteer on a shared device): NEUTRAL-to-reject as a step-up target; it is a session-design question, not a sensitive-operation.** There is no identity-switch feature and no volunteer session in code yet. Re-entering *your* PIN doesn't prove the *previous* volunteer logged out; the honest control is session hygiene (explicit sign-out, short idle timeout), not a re-auth prompt.

I am **not** re-litigating D1 (KILL server-side/persistent face — settled), D2 (GREEN device-key+PIN — settled), D3 / R1-D2 (advisory, action-scoped geofence — settled), or R1-D1 (on-device dedup — settled with conditions). I treat all of those as fixed and reason only about **which volunteer/reporter actions warrant an extra confirmation step, and by what mechanism.**

## Principles Alignment

Applied first to **the question itself** (what should trigger step-up), then to **each starter candidate**. ALIGNED/VIOLATED/NEUTRAL is judged against *whether adding step-up here serves the principle*.

### The question itself — "which actions need an extra confirmation step?"

- **AI recommends, people decide**: **ALIGNED as framed — with one sharp caveat.** A PIN prompt on a genuinely destructive action is a *human* confirming intent before an irreversible effect; that is the principle working. The caveat is entirely about **Candidate 1's mechanism**: if the *server inspects submission content* (`condition=deceased`) and *decides on its own* to demand re-auth, that is a new automated content-judgment inserted into the volunteer's path. It doesn't take a life-affecting *action* (so it's not a clean VIOLATED), but it makes an automated determination *about* sensitivity and imposes a consequence (friction, a stop) from it. The principle's spirit — machines advise, humans decide — is better served by a criterion humans can read off the *action type* (create vs. destroy) than by the server classifying *content* to decide how hard to challenge the user. See C1.
- **Information before interfaces**: **VIOLATED by over-scoping; ALIGNED by the minimal scope.** The point of moving off face-login (D1/D2) was to get *correct field information to coordinators fast*. Every step-up on a **create** directly taxes that goal: it slows the exact low-friction reporting the whole decision family optimized for, and buys nothing, because a create can't corrupt existing information. Friction on *destructive* edits, by contrast, *protects* the information base. So this principle is the one that most cleanly sorts the candidates: guard destruction, never guard creation.
- **Trust & honesty (never record or claim something that did not happen)**: **This is the load-bearing principle, and it cuts two ways.** (a) It *supports* step-up on edit/retract: silently altering a submitted record after the fact is precisely "the record now claims something different from what happened," and a re-auth speed-bump on that is honesty-protective. (b) It *warns against* Candidate 1's content-conditional form: making the system's own auth behavior depend on a server-side reading of `condition` risks teaching volunteers that marking `deceased` triggers extra hurdles — which creates an incentive to mis-mark to avoid friction, actively *degrading* the truthfulness of the single most consequential field. A rule that pressures people to record something *other* than what happened is the exact inversion of this principle. See C2.
- **Data minimization / least-PII**: **NEUTRAL, tilting against content-conditional.** Step-up itself collects no new data (re-entering an already-established PIN). But Candidate 1's mechanism requires the auth layer to *read the submission's clinical content* (`condition`) to make an auth decision — coupling the authentication path to sensitive medical-status content it otherwise has no need to branch on. Minimization prefers an auth trigger keyed on *action type* (create/edit/delete — structural, non-sensitive) over one keyed on *field values* (deceased — sensitive). The content-blind criterion touches no PII to decide; the content-conditional one must inspect the most sensitive field to decide.
- **Auditability (every state change append-only and attributable)**: **ALIGNED — and it quietly REPLACES most of the motivation for step-up.** HOS is already append-only: `intake.ts` writes a `report.created` event, `condition` is already in the audit payload, and there is no mutation path that isn't itself an event (`familyReach.ts`, `verification.ts` all `appendEvent`). On an append-only store, a "compromised session submits a bad report" is a *new attributable event you can see and correct*, not a silent corruption — which sharply lowers the value of step-up on *creates* (the damage is visible and reversible-by-superseding) and concentrates all the real value on *edit/retract*, where the concern is precisely tampering that an append-only log is supposed to prevent. If an edit/retract endpoint is built append-only (old value preserved, change attributed), even that risk shrinks. Auditability is doing a lot of the protective work people are reaching to step-up for.
- **Crisis-grade & reversible**: **ALIGNED with the create-vs-destroy criterion; VIOLATED by broad step-up.** In an active crisis on low-end devices, every extra modal is a place a tired volunteer under a flickering light (the User's Petare scenario, D1) fails, mis-types, or abandons the report. Step-up is justified *only* where the underlying action is itself *irreversible* — which is the same line the criterion draws. Guarding a reversible create fails this principle (friction with no reversibility payoff); guarding an irreversible destroy honors it (the friction is proportionate to the irreversibility). Note the elegant alignment: **the thing that makes an action worth a step-up (irreversibility) is the same property this principle cares about.** That is the tell that irreversibility is the correct single criterion.

### Starter candidates

**Candidate 1 — found-report where `condition=deceased`.**
- AI-recommends/people-decide: **VIOLATED (content-conditional form).** Server reads content, auto-decides to challenge — a new automated content-judgment. Content-blind form is NEUTRAL here but fails on other grounds below.
- Information-before-interfaces: **VIOLATED.** Taxes the fastest-path create HOS is built to protect.
- Trust & honesty: **VIOLATED.** Creates an incentive to mis-mark the most consequential field to dodge friction; and the *legitimate* sensitivity of `deceased` is already honored downstream by `familyReach.ts`/D4, so step-up here is redundant with an existing, better-placed control.
- Data-minimization: **VIOLATED (mild).** Couples auth to the reading of sensitive clinical content.
- Auditability: **NEUTRAL.** The create is already an attributable event regardless of step-up.
- Crisis/reversible: **VIOLATED.** A create is reversible-by-superseding; friction here has no reversibility payoff, only failure risk.
- **Net: REJECT.** The one principle that could support it (trust/honesty) is already satisfied elsewhere and is in fact *harmed* by this mechanism.

**Candidate 2 — edit/retract an existing report (does not exist today).**
- AI-recommends/people-decide: **ALIGNED.** A human re-confirming intent before an irreversible overwrite.
- Information-before-interfaces: **ALIGNED.** Protects the information base coordinators rely on.
- Trust & honesty: **ALIGNED — strongest fit.** Post-hoc silent alteration is the canonical "record now claims something that didn't happen"; a speed-bump is honesty-protective.
- Data-minimization: **NEUTRAL.** Triggers on *action type* (edit/retract), not on content — no sensitive-field inspection needed.
- Auditability: **ALIGNED — provided the edit is itself append-only** (preserve prior value, attribute the change). Step-up + append-only edit together are belt-and-suspenders against tampering.
- Crisis/reversible: **ALIGNED.** The action is destructive/irreversible by nature, so proportionate friction fits.
- **Net: the ONE candidate the criterion admits — but it is a phantom.** Disposition: adopt the *rule* now; the friction is born with the endpoint if/when it exists.

**Candidate 3 — switch active volunteer identity on a shared device (no such feature today).**
- AI-recommends/people-decide: **NEUTRAL.** Switching identity isn't a life-affecting action; it's session state.
- Information-before-interfaces: **NEUTRAL.**
- Trust & honesty: **NEUTRAL-to-AT-RISK.** Re-entering *your own* PIN does not prove the *prior* volunteer intended to hand off or logged out; it can create a false sense that attribution is clean when it isn't. The honest control is explicit sign-out + idle timeout, not a re-auth prompt on the incomer.
- Data-minimization: **NEUTRAL.**
- Auditability: **AT-RISK if mis-modeled.** The real requirement is that each submission is attributed to the *currently* authenticated volunteer — a session-design property, not a step-up.
- Crisis/reversible: **NEUTRAL.** Shift changes are frequent and hurried (the User's shelter scenario); a modal at every handoff is friction on a routine event.
- **Net: NOT a sensitive-operation; it is a session-hygiene question.** Route it to D2's session design (short idle timeout, explicit sign-out, per-submission attribution to the live session), not to this list.

## Logic Check

1. **Claim: "content-conditional step-up (inspect `condition` server-side, prompt only when `deceased`) is a reasonable way to scope sensitivity."** — **Does NOT hold as good design (C1).** Three problems. (a) *It is a new automated content-judgment.* The server reads the clinical field and unilaterally decides to raise a barrier — inserting machine classification of *content* into the human's action path, which sits uneasily with "AI recommends, people decide" even though it stops short of a life-affecting action. (b) *It creates a perverse incentive against honesty.* If `deceased` visibly costs extra friction, the rational tired user mis-marks `unknown`/`injured` to avoid it — degrading the single most consequential field, the exact opposite of the trust-and-honesty principle. (c) *It is redundant with a better-placed existing control.* `familyReach.ts`/`isSensitiveOutcome` (Board D4) already treats `deceased` as sensitive **where sensitivity actually matters** — the human, compassionate contact of the family — and gates *resolution* on a human, not the *reporter* on a machine. Adding reporter-side step-up doesn't add protection; it adds friction and a mis-marking incentive on top of a control that already exists. **Verdict: content-conditional step-up is inconsistent with three of the six principles and duplicative of D4. Reject.**

2. **Claim: "a found-report with `condition=deceased` is 'consequential enough' to warrant extra confirmation."** — **Confuses consequence-of-the-INFORMATION with consequence-of-the-ACTION (C2).** The *information* (someone died) is grave — nobody disputes that, and HOS treats it gravely downstream. But the *action* (appending one immutable report) is neither irreversible nor destructive to any existing record: it creates a new row, already audit-logged, that a coordinator verifies before anything happens to a family (`verification.ts` → `familyReach.ts`, all human-gated). Step-up authenticates the *actor at the moment of an action*; it is the wrong tool for "this information is emotionally heavy." The right tools for heavy information are the ones HOS already has: compassionate downstream handling (D4) and human verification-before-family-contact. **The gravity of `deceased` is real and already honored; it is not an argument for reporter re-auth.** Does not hold.

3. **Claim: "a per-action list is the right shape for defining sensitive operations."** — **Does NOT hold; a single criterion is simpler and more robust (see Simplest Solution).** A hand-maintained list goes stale the moment a new endpoint ships and nobody re-opens this decision; it also invites bikeshedding over each entry. A criterion ("irreversible/destructive to an existing record → step-up; pure create → never") classifies *future* endpoints automatically and encodes *why*. The proposal itself asks for a list but its own three candidates collapse under one rule: create (reject), destroy (accept), session-switch (out of category). That collapse is the evidence the criterion, not the list, is the real answer. Does not hold as the best shape.

4. **Claim: "re-entering the device PIN (not face) is the right mechanism."** — **Holds, conditionally.** Given D1's settled KILL, PIN re-entry is the only mechanism consistent with the record — face in any repeated/step-up capacity is off the table by construction, so the proposal is right not to reopen it. BUT the claim quietly presumes a device-PIN *exists to re-enter*, and **it does not yet** (D2's device-key+PIN volunteer identity is GREEN-LIT but unbuilt; verified — no PIN/session/device-key primitive in the code, only the coordinator token gate). So PIN re-entry is the correct mechanism *for the empty/near-empty scope this round should define*, to be implemented **only alongside** the D2 identity it depends on. Holds as "the right mechanism, for whenever there is anything to gate and a PIN to gate it with" — which is not today.

5. **Claim (implicit in the framing): "some sensitive volunteer operation exists today that needs protecting."** — **Does NOT hold on the verified surface.** The only volunteer-side writes are two append-only creates; every post-creation state change (`matched`, `resolved`) is coordinator-side or the automated matcher — all out of scope. There is no volunteer edit, retract, delete, or identity-switch in the tree. The honest finding is that the problem this round is scoping is **almost entirely anticipatory**: worth *defining a criterion for*, not worth *building friction for*, until the surface actually grows a destructive action. Does not hold for today; holds as future-proofing.

## My Proposed Scope

Stated as a criterion plus its application. For each action, the principle each decision serves:

**The criterion (this is the deliverable):**
> Step-up (device-PIN re-entry) attaches to an action **iff** that action is **irreversible or destructive to an existing record** — it deletes, retracts, or silently overwrites information a coordinator or family already relies on. **Creating a new record never triggers step-up.** The trigger is the **action's structural type (create vs. destroy)**, never a **server-side reading of the submission's content.**

**Application to the verified + foreseeable surface:**

| Action | In scope today? | Step-up? | Which principle the decision serves |
|---|---|---|---|
| `POST /api/found` (incl. `condition=deceased`) | Yes (exists) | **NO** | *Information-before-interfaces* + *crisis/reversible*: it's a reversible, audit-logged create; friction taxes the fast path HOS is built for and buys no reversibility. *Trust/honesty*: avoids a mis-marking incentive on the `deceased` field. |
| `POST /api/missing` | Yes (exists) | **NO** | Same as above — append-only create. |
| Edit / retract an existing report | **No (does not exist)** | **YES, if ever built** | *Trust/honesty* (no silent post-hoc alteration) + *information-before-interfaces* (protect the record base) + *auditability* (pair with append-only edit). This is the sole member of the scope. |
| Hard-delete a report | No (does not exist) | **YES, if ever built** | *Trust/honesty* + *crisis/reversible*: deletion is the maximally irreversible/destructive action; it is the clearest possible member of the criterion. |
| Switch active volunteer on shared device | No (does not exist) | **NO (out of category)** | Routed to D2 session design (explicit sign-out + idle timeout + per-submission attribution), which *serves auditability* better than a re-auth prompt that can't prove the prior user logged out. |

**Additions I make to the starters:** I add **hard-delete** explicitly (the proposal's surface note implies only edit/retract; delete is the sharpest case and should be named so the criterion visibly covers it) and I **reclassify** identity-switch out of "sensitive operation" into "session hygiene" so it isn't mistakenly solved with a step-up.

**Net:** the *list* is empty today; the *criterion* is the artifact; the *first real trigger* is whichever destructive endpoint (edit/retract/delete) is built first, at which point step-up is designed in, not added on.

## Simplest Solution

**Yes — there is a single criterion that beats a per-action list, and it is Occam-preferred by a wide margin: gate on IRREVERSIBILITY, not on content, not on an enumerated list.**

> **Reversible/additive action → no step-up. Irreversible/destructive-to-existing-record action → step-up.**

Why this is strictly simpler *and* better than the alternatives:

- **vs. a per-action list:** a list is O(n) entries that must be *manually* revisited every time the API grows, and it silently rots when someone ships an endpoint without re-opening this decision. The criterion is O(1): it classifies any *future* endpoint automatically and carries its own justification. The proposal's own three candidates reduce to it (create→no, destroy→yes, session-switch→neither), which is the proof the list was the wrong abstraction.
- **vs. content-conditional (`condition=deceased`):** the criterion never inspects a sensitive field to make an auth decision, so it sidesteps the automated-content-judgment problem (C1), the mis-marking incentive (C2), and the auth/PII coupling all at once. It keys on *structural action type* — data the router already knows (which HTTP verb / which handler) — not on clinical content.
- **it self-aligns with the principles:** the property that makes an action worth guarding (irreversibility) is *the same property* the "crisis-grade & reversible" principle already centers. So the criterion isn't an external rule bolted onto HOS's values — it *is* one of HOS's stated values, applied to auth. That is the strongest possible Occam signal: the simplest rule and the principled rule are the same rule.

**What is lost by simplifying:** the ability to say "this *particular* create is special because its *content* is grave." That capability is deliberately given up — and it should be, because (a) HOS already expresses content-gravity in the correct place (`familyReach.ts`/D4, human-gated), and (b) content-based auth triggers create the honesty-degrading incentive to mis-mark. Losing content-conditional step-up is a feature, not a cost.

**One-line rule for the record:** *"You never re-auth to say something new happened; you re-auth to un-say or overwrite something already recorded."*

## Technical Debt Created

- **Anticipatory-mechanism debt — the main risk of this round.** Building any step-up *now* would create a PIN-re-entry flow for (a) a PIN that doesn't exist yet (D2 unbuilt) and (b) an action surface with nothing to gate. That is dead code guarding a phantom, and it would drift out of sync with D2's actual session model when that lands. **Mitigation / owned?** Owned *if* the board adopts "criterion now, code later": define the rule, ship no friction, and bind step-up into the *design* of the first destructive endpoint. If the board instead over-reads this round as "build step-up for deceased-reports," it creates exactly this debt for negative value. Flagging so the round doesn't over-deliver.
- **Criterion-drift / enforced-invariant debt (inherited from D3 and R1).** "Creates never step-up; only destructive actions do" is, like D3's "advisory-never-block" and R1-D1's "always a fallback lane," a rule enforced today only by intent. When an edit/retract endpoint is built, it needs a **test that fails if a create path ever acquires a step-up, and fails if a destructive path lacks one** — otherwise a future capacity-crunch or a well-meaning PR silently inverts the criterion (the same drift vector that let HOS-002's fail-open and false-"sent" bugs in). **Owned?** Not yet — this round should hand the invariant forward as an explicit condition on the first destructive endpoint, mirroring D3's enforced-invariant condition.
- **Redundant-control debt IF Candidate 1 is adopted.** Reporter-side step-up on `deceased` would sit *on top of* the existing `familyReach.ts`/D4 handling, giving two half-overlapping "deceased is special" mechanisms in different layers — future maintainers must then reason about both and keep them consistent. **Owned?** Only avoidable by rejecting Candidate 1, which I recommend. Naming it so the redundancy is a conscious rejection, not an accident.
- **Session-model debt for Candidate 3 (deferred, correctly).** Shared-device handoff is a real operational fact (shelter shift changes, one phone — the User's and R1's scenario), but its correct home is D2's session lifecycle (idle timeout, explicit sign-out, per-submission attribution to the live session), *not* this list. **Owned?** Should be explicitly handed to D2's implementation as a named requirement so it isn't dropped by being ruled out of *this* scope. Deferring the *mechanism* is fine; dropping the *requirement* is not.

## Confidence Score

**0.86.**

High because the two load-bearing findings are checkable facts, not taste: (1) I verified the *entire* volunteer-facing mutation surface is two append-only creates with **no** edit/retract/delete/identity-switch endpoint anywhere in the tree (`grep` for `PUT|PATCH|DELETE` in the found/missing routes returns nothing; every post-creation status write is coordinator-side or the automated matcher), so "which sensitive volunteer operation exists today?" has the concrete answer *none* — which is what collapses the per-action list into "empty today." (2) I verified HOS *already* encodes `deceased`-sensitivity at the correct layer (`familyReach.ts:34` `isSensitiveOutcome`, Board D4, human-gated resolution), so Candidate 1 is not just principle-inconsistent but *duplicative of an existing, better-placed control* — a stronger objection than "it adds friction." The criterion (irreversibility, not content) then follows directly from HOS's own six principles — most cleanly from "crisis-grade & reversible," whose central property *is* irreversibility — so it is the project's logic applied, not my preference. The content-conditional rejection rests on three independent principle violations (C1) plus the honesty-degrading mis-marking incentive (C2), which is a convergent, not a single-thread, argument.

Not higher because: (a) whether HOS *should* build an edit/retract endpoint at all is a genuine product question I take no position on — I only pre-commit the rule for *if* it does; the actual risk model for post-hoc tampering (how often, by whom, to what end) is the Contrarian's lane, not mine, and would sharpen how urgent that endpoint's step-up is. (b) The device-PIN mechanism's real usability on the low-end fleet (does a second PIN prompt actually get completed under a flickering Petare light, or abandoned?) is the User's and Researcher's lane — my recommendation *reduces* exposure to this by scoping step-up to rare destructive actions, but doesn't eliminate it. (c) I cannot rule out a *future* non-CRUD volunteer action (e.g. a "close/withdraw my own report" affordance) that is destructive without being a classic edit — the criterion is designed to catch it, but I haven't enumerated every conceivable future affordance, only shown the criterion classifies the foreseeable ones correctly.

## What I Don't Know

- **Whether HOS will ever build a volunteer-facing edit/retract/delete endpoint.** That is a product decision upstream of this one. My scope pre-commits the rule but cannot force or forbid the endpoint. If the answer is "never," the sensitive-operations list stays *permanently* empty for volunteers and this round's only durable output is the criterion — which is fine.
- **The real post-hoc-tampering threat rate** — how likely a compromised/coerced volunteer session is to be used to *alter* a record (vs. file a bad new one), and to what operational harm. This sizes how urgent step-up-on-edit actually is. It is the Contrarian's failure-scenario lane; my role is to say *which* actions clear a principled bar, not to quantify the attack frequency.
- **Whether re-entering an already-established device PIN is genuinely completable on the documented low-end Android fleet under field conditions**, or whether even a rare destructive-action prompt becomes an abandonment point. My criterion minimizes how often this is hit; the User/Researcher own whether the mechanism is usable when it is hit.
- **What D2's session model will actually look like** (idle timeout length, explicit sign-out UX, per-submission attribution) — Candidate 3 (shared-device handoff) should be solved *there*, but that design isn't built yet, so I can hand it the requirement but not verify the solution.
- **Whether any near-term feature will introduce a destructive volunteer action that isn't a classic CRUD edit** (e.g. "withdraw my report," "merge duplicates"). The criterion is built to catch these by their irreversibility regardless of shape, but I have verified the criterion only against today's surface plus the obvious edit/retract/delete, not an exhaustive future roadmap.
