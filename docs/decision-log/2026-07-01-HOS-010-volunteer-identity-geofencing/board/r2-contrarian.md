# Contrarian Review: HOS-2026-010-R2 (defining sensitive operations)

> Round 2. Identity (D2: device-key + PIN) and geofencing (D3/R1-D2: action-scoped, advisory-only) are SETTLED and I am not reopening them. This round is scope: which volunteer/reporter actions deserve an extra confirmation step, and does the starter list have the right things on it. I verified the action surface myself in the code before writing this — see the receipts inline.

## My Recommendation

**RESHAPE THE LIST — hard.** Specifically: **cut it to ONE item, reframe that item, and defer the mechanism.**

The starter list has three candidates. After reading the actual code, my verdict per candidate is:

1. **`condition = deceased` step-up → REMOVE.** Content-conditional step-up on a single anonymous create call is the most complexity-per-unit-safety on the list, protects almost nothing, and introduces a new automated-content-judgment behavior the project's own principles are wary of. This is padding.
2. **Edit/retract step-up → REMOVE FROM SCOPE (as written), REPLACE with a design constraint.** You cannot put a step-up gate on an endpoint that does not exist. Worse, defining "sensitive" against a hypothetical endpoint invites someone to *build the endpoint* to satisfy the checklist. The correct output here is a **rule that the edit/retract endpoint, if ever built, must not mutate the original record** — which makes the step-up question largely moot by construction.
3. **Shared-device identity switch → KEEP — this is the only real one — but the framing is wrong.** The risk is not "someone maliciously switches identity." The risk is **silent misattribution**: the phone still has volunteer A active, volunteer B grabs it mid-shift and files three reports, and now the append-only log permanently and wrongly attributes B's reports to A. That is the one place on this list where an ounce of friction buys something that cannot be undone later.

And the thing that scares me most is **not on the list at all**, because the proposal framed the whole exercise as "which actions need step-up" and skipped the prior question: **there is no identity to step up yet, and the highest-consequence failure is a compromised/borrowed device signing reports low-friction — which no per-action PIN prompt fixes.** More below.

---

## Did the proposal ask the right question? (the meta-flaw)

The proposal says day-to-day submission "should stay low-friction — that is the whole point of moving away from face-based login," then asks which submissions deserve *added* friction. That framing quietly assumes the interesting security event is **a legitimate user doing a consequential thing**. It is not. The interesting security event is **the wrong principal at the keyboard** — a seized phone, a borrowed phone, a coerced volunteer, a shared shelter device with the wrong session active. Step-up-on-sensitive-action is a defense tuned for the first threat and nearly useless against the second, because whoever holds the unlocked device also holds (or can extract) the PIN. R1-D2's own condition already conceded this: *"a PIN can still be extracted under pressure … data-minimization on what a compromised account actually exposes … is the mitigation, not an unattainable duress-proof login."* This round is at risk of re-litigating that conceded point under a new name.

That does not make step-up worthless — it makes **exactly one** of its use cases real (misattribution on a shared device, where the "attacker" is just an honest volunteer in a hurry, and a prompt genuinely helps). The rest is friction cosplaying as security.

## Fatal Flaws Found

### Flaw 1: You cannot step-up-authenticate an identity that does not exist yet — this whole round is gated on unbuilt, unsequenced work
**Severity:** CRITICAL (to the premise, not to any one candidate)
**What breaks:** I grepped the codebase. There is **no device-key, no PIN, no volunteer/reporter identity, no session** anywhere in `apps/web/app` today. `POST /api/found` (`apps/web/app/api/found/route.ts`) and `POST /api/missing` (`apps/web/app/api/missing/route.ts`) are **anonymous public POSTs** — rate-limited, schema-validated, and that's it. Identity is a free-text `reporter_org` / `reporter_name` / `reporter_contact` string (`apps/web/app/lib/db/schema.ts:49-51`), self-asserted, unverified, unauthenticated.
**Why it's real:** "Step up" means "prove again you are the enrolled principal." There is no enrolled principal. D2 (device-key+PIN) is GREEN_LIT but explicitly sequenced **AFTER HOS-2026-001-08** ("do not build a second, parallel volunteer identity system … the account record this rests on should BE that system's volunteer/reporter role"), and that auth work has not landed. So every candidate on this list is really "a thing we will do to an identity layer that does not exist, that sits on an auth substrate that does not exist."
**Impact:** If the board "defines the sensitive-operations list" now as if it were implementable, it will be treated as a spec, and someone will try to satisfy it before the substrate it depends on is real — repeating precisely the "don't build the feature on an unaudited, in-progress auth substrate" risk that D2's conditions were written to prevent. The honest output of this round is a **policy that activates when the identity layer ships**, not a build ticket.
**Could we fix it?** YES, for free, by scoping the deliverable correctly: define the *principle* (which actions warrant step-up, and why), explicitly mark it **dormant until D2's identity layer exists**, and forbid implementing any step-up check before the thing it steps up *to* is built and audited. Zero cost; it just requires not pretending the substrate is here.

### Flaw 2: Content-conditional step-up on `condition = deceased` is complexity buying almost nothing, and it introduces automated content-judgment the project distrusts
**Severity:** HIGH (as a reason to REMOVE candidate 1)
**What breaks:** The proposal itself names the problem: deceased is *one field value inside one create call* (`condition TEXT ... DEFAULT 'unknown'`, `apps/web/app/lib/db/schema.ts:47`; the enum lives in `IntakeForms.tsx:31` and validation in `foundReportSchema`). To do "step up only when deceased," the server must **inspect the submission body, branch on its content, and interrupt the flow to demand PIN re-entry** before accepting an otherwise-normal create.
**Why it's real:** Walk the failure it's supposed to prevent. A volunteer at a morgue/shelter marks a found person `deceased`. Without step-up: the report is created, attributed, timestamped, append-logged, and — critically — **coordinators still verify every match by hand before anyone acts** (that's an existing board condition; `apps/web/app/lib/coordination/match.ts` comment: *"a human always decides"*). A wrong `deceased` is caught the same way a wrong `alive` is: by human verification and corroboration. So what does the PIN prompt actually prevent? Essentially: a malicious holder of an unlocked, enrolled device from filing *specifically a deceased report* — but that same holder can file a false `alive`/`hospitalized` report, or a false missing report, with **no** friction. You've hardened one enum value out of five and left the barn door open. The consequence profile of a false `deceased` (distress to a family) is real and awful, but it is delivered through the **notification/verification path**, which is coordinator-gated, not through the raw submission.
**The second-order harm:** content-conditional step-up means **the system reads what you typed and decides your submission is suspicious.** The proposal's own question to Principals flags this: is inspecting submission content server-side to gate an action consistent with "AI recommends, people decide" and "trust and honesty"? I'll answer from the adversarial seat: it sets a precedent that the intake layer makes automated judgments about the *content* of a humanitarian report and adds friction based on them. Today it's `deceased`. Tomorrow it's a keyword filter, a "this looks like a duplicate" gate, a "this location is sensitive" gate. Each is individually defensible and collectively becomes exactly the kind of opaque automated gatekeeping this project positions itself against. A **content-blind** rule ("no create call ever triggers step-up") has none of this baggage.
**Could we fix it?** N/A — the fix is to **not do it.** Remove candidate 1.

### Flaw 3: Defining step-up for a non-existent edit/retract endpoint invites building the endpoint, and misses the real control (immutability)
**Severity:** HIGH (as a reason to RESHAPE candidate 2 into a constraint, not a step-up rule)
**What breaks:** I confirmed there is **no edit, update, retract, PATCH, or DELETE** endpoint for either report type — only `GET` (list) and `POST` (create) exist in `apps/web/app/api/found/route.ts` and `.../missing/route.ts`. The candidate is explicitly conditional ("IF such an endpoint is ever built").
**Why it's real (two ways):**
1. **Checklist-driven scope creep.** If "editing/retracting a report is a sensitive operation requiring step-up" lands in the decision log as a defined sensitive op, the natural reading downstream is "we have decided reports are editable, and the edit endpoint needs a PIN gate." That is a latent authorization to build a mutation surface the system deliberately does not have today. The current design is **append-only by construction** — `events` is documented as *"append-only: code only ever INSERTs into it"* (`schema.ts:9`), and reports are create-then-verify. Introducing edit/retract is a *much* bigger decision than "does it need step-up," and burying it as an implied premise of a step-up candidate is how a consequential architecture change sneaks in through the side door.
2. **Even if you build it, step-up is the wrong primary control.** The stated concern is "a chance to quietly tamper with a record after the fact." A PIN prompt does not stop tampering — the authenticated user *is* the one tampering, and re-entering their own PIN is no obstacle to them. The control that actually addresses "quietly tamper after the fact" is **never mutating the original**: an "edit" appends a correction event; a "retract" appends a tombstone/withdrawal event; the original report and its full history remain in the append-only log and visible to coordinators. Tampering becomes structurally impossible to do *quietly* because nothing is ever overwritten — which is already the system's design philosophy.
**Impact:** Frame this as a step-up candidate and you get (a) pressure to build a mutation endpoint, (b) a PIN gate that doesn't stop the named threat, and (c) an original record that can now be silently altered. Frame it as an immutability constraint and the threat evaporates without any per-action auth theater.
**Could we fix it?** YES. Replace candidate 2 with a **design rule**: "No edit/retract endpoint exists today and none should be built under this decision. IF a future decision authorizes correction/withdrawal of a submitted report, it MUST be append-only (correction/tombstone events over the immutable original), never an in-place mutation. Step-up on such an action is at most a secondary confirmation, not the primary integrity control." That kills the tamper threat by construction and stops the scope creep.

### Flaw 4: The one real candidate (shared-device switch) is on the list for the wrong reason, and left as-is it protects the wrong thing
**Severity:** HIGH (KEEP the candidate, FIX the framing)
**What breaks:** Candidate 3 — switching the active volunteer identity on a shared shelter phone. The proposal frames it under "abuse of a compromised session." That framing under-sells it and mis-aims it.
**Why it's real (concrete scenario):** Petare shelter, one Tecno Spark on the front desk, three volunteers across a night shift (this is the exact device/context User grounded in the parent D1 review, and R1-D1 flagged shared-device accumulation as unresolved). Volunteer A logs in at 20:00. At 23:00 A goes to sleep without logging out. B picks up the phone and files four found-person reports over the next two hours. **Every one is attributed to A in the append-only `events` log (`actor` column, `schema.ts:152`), permanently.** No malice — B was just working. But now: (a) provenance is wrong and *uncorrectable* (append-only means you can't quietly fix the attribution later), (b) if A's account is ever compromised, coerced, or investigated, A carries responsibility for reports A never made, and (c) the "trusted provenance layer" that D2 exists to create is silently corrupted at the exact seam — the shared device — the whole population runs on.
**Why the proposal's framing is wrong:** "compromised session" implies an attacker. The dangerous-and-common case has **no attacker** — it's an honest volunteer in a hurry on a shared device. That reframing matters because it changes what "step-up" is *for*: not to stop a bad actor (a bad actor holding the phone wins regardless), but to **force an explicit, logged handoff of attribution** so the record stays honest. That is squarely in-bounds for a project whose north star is trustworthy provenance.
**What the step-up moment must actually be (or it's useless):** the friction has to land at **identity switch**, and — because a shift-change volunteer is always in a hurry — the default has to fail toward *correct attribution*, not toward convenience. Minimum viable version: on a device flagged "shared," submitting a report **re-prompts for the active volunteer's PIN if the session is older than the shift window / went idle**, and switching users is an explicit PIN-in action that writes a handoff event. If the prompt is skippable or the session just silently persists, misattribution happens anyway and the candidate delivers nothing.
**Could we fix it?** YES — but note the honest limit: this only works if the device *knows* it's shared (a per-device "shared" mode) and if the enrolled-identity layer from D2 exists. Both are prerequisites, reinforcing Flaw 1. On a personal single-user phone, this candidate should **not** fire — mandatory step-up there is pure friction with no misattribution risk to prevent.

### Flaw 5: PIN re-entry is the least-bad mechanism inside the settled boundary — but don't oversell it, and watch the duress footgun
**Severity:** MEDIUM
**What breaks:** The proposal proposes PIN re-entry as the step-up mechanism (correctly ruling out face, since any repeated/persistent face use recreates the D1-killed registry/coercion risk — that boundary is settled and I'm not touching it). I agree PIN is the only mechanism left standing. But two cautions:
1. **A re-entered PIN proves possession of the PIN, not presence of the enrolled human.** On a shared/seized device the PIN is available to whoever holds it. So PIN step-up genuinely helps the **honest-misattribution** case (Flaw 4) and helps essentially nothing in the **hostile-holder** case. Sell it as the former only. Claiming it as a defense against coercion or seizure would repeat the project's own D4 honesty failure (don't claim a control does something it doesn't).
2. **Duress footgun:** adding a "confirm this sensitive action with your PIN" step at a morgue/deceased moment creates a scripted, predictable point where a volunteer is compelled to enter their PIN — which, per R1-D2's duress condition, is a value an adversary standing over them can then observe/extract. A step-up prompt that fires *specifically* on the most sensitive submissions is, perversely, a PIN-harvesting prompt at the worst moment. This is a second, independent reason to kill the `condition=deceased` step-up (Flaw 2): it's not just low-value, it's mildly *counter*-productive to the duress posture D2 was told to protect.
**Could we fix it?** Partly. Keep PIN re-entry ONLY for the identity-handoff/idle-shared-session case, where its "prove you hold the PIN" semantics actually match the goal (correct attribution), and where the person is not being singled out by report content. Do not attach it to content of a submission.

## My Proposed Scope

**Actions I believe should be "sensitive" (i.e. warrant step-up):**

- **Active-identity switch, and submission on a stale/idle session, on a device in "shared" mode.**
  - *Concrete failure if unprotected:* the shared-shelter misattribution walkthrough in Flaw 4 — honest volunteer B's reports permanently and uncorrectably logged as A's, corrupting the provenance layer at the one seam the whole system depends on, and exposing A to responsibility for reports A never filed.
  - *Severity: HIGH.*
  - *Mechanism:* PIN re-entry to switch users / to submit after idle-on-shared; writes an explicit handoff event. Fails toward correct attribution, not convenience. **Only on shared-mode devices; never on a personal single-user device.**

**Starter candidates I would REMOVE or downgrade:**

- **Candidate 1 (`condition = deceased` step-up): REMOVE.** Hardens one enum value of five while leaving false `alive`/`hospitalized`/missing reports frictionless; the real safety net (human verification before action, coordinator-gated) already catches wrong-condition reports; content-conditional gating introduces automated content-judgment the project distrusts; and it creates a PIN-harvest-at-worst-moment duress footgun. Highest complexity, near-zero marginal safety.
- **Candidate 2 (edit/retract step-up): REMOVE AS A STEP-UP RULE; REPLACE with an immutability constraint.** No such endpoint exists; naming it "sensitive" invites building it; and even built, a PIN gate doesn't stop the authenticated user from tampering. The real control is append-only correction/tombstone events over an immutable original — which the codebase's philosophy already favors (`events` is insert-only). Step-up there is at most secondary.

**Action NOT in the starter list that I believe belongs there (or above it):**

- **The prerequisite, not an action: "there is no identity to protect yet."** The single most consequential failure — a borrowed/seized enrolled device signing reports at normal friction — is not solved by any per-action step-up (holder has the PIN). The board should state plainly that (a) this scope is **dormant until D2's identity layer + HOS-2026-001-08 auth land**, and (b) the primary compensating control for a compromised device is **data-minimization on what a signed session can even do** (submission-signing only, per D2's condition), not step-up. If anything is "missing from the list," it's the honest acknowledgment that the list's premise outruns the built system.

## Edge Cases We Haven't Addressed

1. **Anonymous path vs. step-up.** D2 mandates a genuinely anonymous submission path survives ("enrollment must not become a precondition for helping at all"). An anonymous submitter has no PIN. So step-up is **undefined for anonymous submissions by construction** — meaning any "sensitive action" that anonymous users can perform *cannot* be gated this way. If `deceased` reports can be filed anonymously (they can today), candidate 1 is not just low-value, it's **inapplicable to half the population**. Another nail in candidate 1.
2. **Idle timeout on a cracked, low-end Android that aggressively kills background state.** If the "stale session → re-prompt" logic depends on reliable background timers/storage on the exact budget-Android fleet R1-D2 flagged as "genuinely spotty," the shared-device protection may misfire (constant re-prompts) or fail silently (never re-prompts). Prototype on real hardware before committing, same as D2's WebAuthn condition.
3. **Volunteer forgets PIN mid-shift at the exact step-up moment.** If step-up hard-blocks and there's no fallback, you've reproduced the false-lockout failure mode the whole face-KILL was partly about — now via PIN. The report doesn't get filed. Needs a graceful fallback (e.g. fall back to the anonymous/unattributed lane rather than losing the report).
4. **First-ever "sensitive" action after fresh enroll, before the user has ever exercised the PIN.** Muscle-memory isn't there; failure/lockout rate is highest exactly when trust is lowest.
5. **Batch entry.** A volunteer entering 15 people at a shelter intake table. If step-up fires per-report (even just on shared mode), 15 PIN entries is not "a moment of friction," it's a workflow killer that pushes people back to paper. Step-up cadence (per-session vs per-action) is unspecified and matters more than the list.

## Questions for the Proposer

1. Since no PIN/identity/session exists in the code today, is the deliverable of this round a **dormant policy** that activates when D2 lands, or is someone expected to implement step-up before the identity layer + HOS-2026-001-08 exist? (If the latter, that violates D2's own sequencing condition.)
2. Can a false `deceased` report reach a family through **any** path that is not coordinator-verified first? If no, what does content-gated step-up on `deceased` protect that human verification doesn't already?
3. Can `condition=deceased` reports be filed on the **anonymous** path (they can today)? If yes, how can PIN step-up gate them at all — and if it can't, why is candidate 1 on the list?
4. Does naming "edit/retract" a sensitive operation constitute authorization to **build** an edit/retract endpoint? If not, will the decision explicitly say "no mutation endpoint under this decision; corrections are append-only if ever built"?
5. For the shared-device case: does the system have (or plan) a way to know a device is **shared vs personal**? Without it, step-up either over-fires (friction everywhere) or under-fires (misattribution persists).
6. What is the intended step-up **cadence** — once per session, per idle-resume, or per sensitive action? The batch-intake edge case (15 people at one table) makes this the difference between "fine" and "volunteers go back to paper."
7. What is the fallback when a volunteer **fails/forgets** the PIN at a step-up moment — hard block (reproducing the lockout failure the face-KILL feared) or graceful fall-through to the anonymous lane?

## Confidence Score

**0.82** — I'm highly confident on the code facts (verified: two create-only endpoints, no edit/retract, no identity/PIN/session, anonymous today, append-only events) and on the reshape (cut to one item, reframe it, defer mechanism). Not higher because the one candidate I keep — shared-device attribution — rests on a genuinely unresolved prerequisite (can the system tell shared from personal, on this hardware, reliably?) that I can't settle from the code alone, and because I'm making a judgment call that coordinator verification fully neutralizes false-`deceased` harm, which depends on no future auto-notify path bypassing verification.

## Final Note

The proposal asked "which actions need step-up" and the honest adversarial answer is "you're guarding the wrong thing on two of three, guarding a thing that doesn't exist on the third, and the whole list is dormant until an identity layer you haven't built shows up." Keep exactly one item — shared-device attribution, reframed as *honest misattribution* rather than *malicious session abuse* — because it's the only place a moment of friction buys something the append-only log can't give back later; make everything else a constraint (immutability) or a deletion (content-gated deceased), and stamp the whole thing "dormant until D2 exists."
