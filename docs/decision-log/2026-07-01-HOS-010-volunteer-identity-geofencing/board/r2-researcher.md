# Researcher Review: HOS-2026-010-R2 (defining sensitive operations)

*Scope-definition round: which volunteer/reporter actions warrant a step-up confirmation beyond day-to-day PIN login.*
Reviewer: Researcher Agent · Date: 2026-07-01 · Method: live web research (banking/fintech step-up triggers, OWASP ASVS 2.26 + OWASP MASWE-0029, NIST SP 800-63B session/reauth rules, retail-POS and healthcare shared-device patterns, humanitarian/military casualty-recording verification norms) + re-read of `addendum-r2-proposal.yaml`, my own `board/r1-researcher.md`, and the settled R1 constraints.

> **What I'm NOT re-opening.** R1 settled it: no server-side or repeated/persistent face use (KILL stands — a re-scan-on-sensitive-action would recreate exactly that), device-key+PIN is the day-to-day mechanism, geo is action-scoped and advisory-only. So the *mechanism* for any step-up here is **re-entering the already-established device PIN** — I found nothing in the comparables that argues for a stronger factor given HOS's threat model (a stronger factor mostly buys resistance to remote credential theft; HOS's dominant threat is *device seizure + compulsion*, which no additional on-device factor defends against, and biometrics actively worsen). This review is only about **scope**: which actions clear the bar.

## My Recommendation

**Adopt a deliberately SMALL scope, grounded in the one pattern every comparable actually shares, and reject the two candidates that don't match it.**

The real-world rule underneath banking, OWASP, and NIST is narrower and more consistent than "high-consequence action." It is: **re-authenticate before an operation that (a) mutates existing state — security settings, an existing record, or account control — where (b) a hijacked/borrowed session is the specific thing you're defending against, and where (c) the operation is not the routine thing the session exists to do.** A first-time create of new content almost never triggers step-up in these systems; mutation of *existing* state, change of *who is acting*, and change of *security/recovery attributes* are what trigger it.

Mapped onto HOS's *verified* action surface (two create-only endpoints, no edit/retract, plus a shared-device reality):

1. **Submitting a found-person report with `condition = deceased` → DO NOT gate with step-up. Recommend NO step-up on any single create call, deceased or otherwise.** This is a first-time create, not a mutation of existing state, and it is precisely the routine action the volunteer's session exists to perform. No comparable I found re-authenticates a user for the *content of a first submission*. The real risk here — a wrong or malicious "deceased" entry harming a family — is a **data-quality / verification** problem, and the entire humanitarian + military casualty-recording literature treats it that way (corroboration, second-party verification, "unverified" labelling), **not** as an authentication problem. Step-up on this field would be solving a data-integrity concern with the wrong tool, add friction to the single most emotionally loaded submission a volunteer makes, and — because it requires the server to inspect submission content to decide whether to challenge — introduce automated content-judgment into the auth path, which sits awkwardly with "AI recommends, people decide." **A plain, always-present in-form confirmation ("You're reporting this person as deceased — confirm") is the right control, and it is not authentication; it's error-prevention. I'd endorse that; I would not call it step-up and would not wire it to the PIN.**

2. **Edit / retract of an already-submitted report → step-up IS warranted *if and only if* such an endpoint is ever built. It does not exist today, so this is a conditional/forward rule, not something to implement now.** This is the one candidate that cleanly matches the comparables: it mutates existing state, it's exactly the "quietly tamper with the record after the fact" scenario a borrowed/hijacked session enables, and it is not the routine create the session exists for. This is the direct analog of "modify an existing payee" or "change a saved record," which banking systems do step up. **Recommendation: bind step-up to the *edit/retract* capability the moment it is designed — but write it as a standing rule now so it isn't forgotten, not as a feature to build ahead of the endpoint.**

3. **Switching which volunteer identity is 'active' on a shared device → YES, confirm identity at the switch — but frame it correctly.** This is the strongest match to real shared-device practice, and it's *not* "step-up authentication" in the banking sense; it's **establish-who-is-now-acting** on a handoff, which retail POS and shared-clinical-tablet guidance treat as mandatory for one reason: **shared/blurred logins destroy the audit trail of who did what.** The value isn't stopping an attacker; it's attribution and honesty of the record. Concretely: a device-level "who's on shift now?" confirm at handoff (pick identity → enter that identity's PIN once to activate the session), *not* a per-submission challenge once someone is active. That keeps day-to-day friction at zero while making each report attributable to the person who actually filed it.

**Net proposed scope: (1) NO auth step-up on submissions (add a non-auth confirm on deceased); (2) a standing rule that step-up attaches to any future edit/retract endpoint; (3) an identity-confirm at shared-device handoff, understood as attribution, not attack-defense. Mechanism throughout: the existing device PIN.** This is smaller than the starter list and, I'll argue below, more defensible than a broader one.

## Comparable Systems

- **Retail bank / mobile banking apps:** step-up (OTP, passkey, biometric prompt *after* login) fires on **high-value transfers**, **adding/modifying a payee**, and **changing security/recovery attributes** (password, email, phone, address). A small transfer or a balance check does *not* step up; the same app steps up a large one or a new payee. → The trigger is *value/mutation of who-can-be-paid or how-recovery-works*, not "using the app." [AuthX; Ping Identity; Entrust; Auth0]
- **OWASP ASVS 2.26 ("Sensitive operations are sufficiently protected"):** *"Verify re-authentication, step up or adaptive authentication, two factor authentication, or transaction signing is required before any application-specific sensitive operations are permitted **as per the risk profile of the application**."* Crucially, the standard **declines to define "sensitive operation"** — it explicitly delegates that to each app's risk profile. → There is no external list to copy; HOS must define its own, which is exactly what this round is for, and ASVS's own worked examples are all *mutations of security/recovery state*, not first-time content creation. [OWASP AASVS 2.26; OWASP ASVS #2727]
- **OWASP Authentication Cheat Sheet / ASVS 3.7:** *"require re-authentication before allowing modifications to sensitive account attributes… email address, phone number, MFA configuration, or other information used in account recovery"* and *"before performing highly sensitive transactions or operations."* Rationale stated verbatim: *"a session-hijack attacker with a stolen cookie should not be able to silently disable MFA."* → The defended-against actor is a **session hijacker**, and the protected verbs are **modify / disable / export / transfer** — all mutations of existing state. [OWASP Authentication Cheat Sheet]
- **OWASP MASWE-0029 (mobile, "Step-Up Authentication Not Implemented After Login"):** canonical example is *logged-in bank user requests a large transfer → must re-authenticate*. Listed sensitive ops: **large transfer, changing account-recovery info, accessing sensitive employee/salary data, finalizing a high-value purchase (re-enter password/CVV).** → Again: value + mutation of recovery/security state, never "created their first record." [OWASP MAS MASWE-0029]
- **NIST SP 800-63B (session & reauthentication):** step-up is defined narrowly as raising a session's **AAL** when a higher assurance level is required for the next operation. Separately, its reauth/timeout rules (AAL1 ≤30 days; AAL2 ≤24h + ≤1h inactivity; AAL3 ≤12h + 15-min inactivity, both factors) exist for a specific reason it states plainly: *"to mitigate situations where a user's device is left logged in but unattended… if the user walks away from a terminal."* → NIST's own justification for re-prompting is **the unattended/shared-device scenario** — which is candidate #3 here, and is *not* the submission-content scenario in #1. This is the single most on-point standard for HOS's shared-shelter-phone reality. [NIST SP 800-63B §Session Management; NIST 800-63-3 AAL Implementation Resources]
- **Retail POS manager-approval workflows (the closest analog to identity-switch on a shared terminal):** industry guidance is blunt — *"Shared PINs destroy accountability… it renders any form of effective POS audit logging useless because you can never be certain who performed a specific action."* Consequential actions (large void/refund, mid-shift drawer open) require a **manager PIN or RFID swipe**, and the system **records who approved and when**; low-value actions are unrestricted. → The controlling principle is **attribution**, and step-up is reserved for **mutations/overrides**, not routine sales. This directly supports (a) confirming identity at handoff and (b) *not* gating routine creates. [HostMerchantServices; ROLLER; Eposly; GoFTX]
- **Shared clinical tablets / healthcare kiosk mode:** guidance is that *"credentials and sessions must not persist beyond the intended clinical task… authenticate the user, constrain the session, and release access immediately after the task ends"*; the named failure mode is *"unattended handoff between staff… the same device may move between departments."* → Same lesson as POS and NIST: the risk that justifies re-confirmation is **handoff on a shared device**, not the content of any one entry. [ManageEngine Endpoint Central; NHIMG shared clinical device]
- **Humanitarian / military casualty & missing-persons recording (the closest analog to a "deceased" entry):** OHCHR casualty-recording and US Army AR 638-8 both handle death data as a **verification / data-quality** discipline — *"accuracy of casualty data must be verified by a field-grade officer,"* units *"verify information… before forwarding,"* and platforms like Ushahidi/ICRC RFL rely on **corroboration and labelled-unverified** handling. **None of these treats submitting death information as an authentication event.** → The strongest available evidence that candidate #1 is a data-integrity problem, not a step-up problem. [OHCHR Guidance on Casualty Recording; AR 638-8; ICRC Central Tracing Agency / RFL; ResearchGate — information verification during disasters]
- **ODK / KoboToolbox (the field-reporting tooling HOS most resembles):** the sector's error-prevention primitive for consequential entries is a **required in-form confirmation / acknowledgement field and constraints**, *not* re-authentication — I found **no** field-reporting tool that re-authenticates a contributor based on the *content* of a submission. → Supports "add a confirm, don't add auth" for the deceased field. [ODK Docs — filling forms / Central submissions; SurveyCTO — validation constraints]

## Ground-Truth Data

- **Banking step-up threshold is value/mutation-based, not action-based:** worked example across sources — *"an individual transferring \$20 online might not… but a \$2000 payment would trigger the extra check,"* and *"only need to authenticate further when they set up a new payee."* (source; the exact figures are illustrative) → the line is drawn at **magnitude + mutation of who-can-be-paid**, and routine activity stays frictionless. [AuthX; Ping Identity]
- **OWASP ASVS 2.26 deliberately provides no canonical "sensitive operation" list** — it delegates entirely to per-app risk profile. → HOS cannot outsource this list; **[fact, high confidence]**. [OWASP AASVS 2.26]
- **NIST reauth exists specifically for unattended/shared terminals**, not for content sensitivity — its own stated rationale. → the standard *points at* candidate #3 and *away from* candidate #1. **[fact, high confidence]**. [NIST SP 800-63B]
- **Shared/duplicated login is the named killer of audit trails** in both POS and clinical guidance ("destroy accountability," "can never be certain who performed a specific action"). → the concrete, evidence-backed reason to confirm identity at handoff is **attribution**, and its value is independent of any attacker model. **[fact, high confidence]**. [GoFTX; ManageEngine]
- **HOS's actual mutation surface today = zero:** two create-only endpoints, no edit/retract/update ([verified in `addendum-r2-proposal.yaml`, this session]). → the *one* candidate that matches the comparables' "mutate existing state" trigger **does not exist yet**, so today's correct step-up scope for submissions is **empty**. **[fact, high confidence — verified against the proposal's action-surface note; I did not independently re-grep the codebase this round]**.
- **Step-up on the deceased field requires server-side content inspection to decide when to challenge** — a design consequence flagged in the proposal itself. → this is a real added-complexity + governance cost (auth path now reads submission content), weighing against candidate #1 beyond just "friction." **[fact]**. ## My Proposed Scope

Grounded in the comparables above — where the tested trigger is *mutation of existing state / change of who-is-acting / high-value transaction*, and first-time content creation is not a step-up event:

**IN scope for PIN step-up:**
- **Edit or retraction of an already-submitted found/missing report** — *conditional on such an endpoint existing.* None does today, so this is written now as a **standing rule** ("any edit/retract capability ships with device-PIN step-up") and implemented only when the endpoint is. This is the sole candidate that matches the comparables' mutation trigger and the session-hijacker rationale.

**IN scope, but as identity-attribution (not attack-defense), at the handoff moment only:**
- **Switching the active volunteer identity on a shared device** — a "who's on shift now?" confirm (select identity → enter that identity's PIN once to activate the session). Justified by POS/clinical/NIST evidence that shared logins destroy attribution and that unattended handoff is *the* shared-device risk. Bounded to the switch, so per-submission friction stays zero.

**OUT of scope for step-up (handle differently):**
- **Submitting any report, including `condition = deceased`** — no auth step-up. It's a routine first-time create, and no comparable gates that on content. **Add a plain, always-present in-form confirmation on the deceased selection** ("You're reporting this person as deceased — confirm"), understood as **error-prevention, not authentication**, not wired to the PIN, and not requiring server-side content inspection. The genuine risk (wrong/malicious deceased entry) belongs to the **data-quality/verification layer** (corroboration, coordinator review, "unverified" handling), consistent with how OHCHR/ICRC/military casualty recording actually works.

**Mechanism for all step-up above:** the **existing device PIN** — no new factor, no face, nothing server-stored, consistent with the settled R1 KILL.

## Unknown Unknowns

- **Whether HOS will ever build edit/retract at all.** My "conditional" recommendation is only load-bearing if that endpoint materializes. If the project deliberately stays append-only forever (which has real integrity advantages for a crisis record), then candidate #2 is moot and the entire step-up scope for *reports* collapses to zero — a legitimate and arguably cleaner outcome. I can't tell from here which way the roadmap goes.
- **What "active identity on a shared device" even means in HOS's build.** I'm assuming a shared shelter phone with multiple registered volunteers and an explicit switch action. If the real deployment is one-device-per-volunteer (personal phones), candidate #3 is also moot and the identity-confirm is unnecessary. The proposal treats shared-device as a live scenario; whether it's the *common* case is unverified.
- **Whether a "confirm deceased" dialog helps or harms in this specific population.** My confidence that an in-form confirm is the right control for candidate #1 is borrowed from ODK/Kobo and general error-prevention UX; I have **no** field data on whether an extra tap on the most painful submission a volunteer makes is experienced as care or as friction/insult in a Venezuelan crisis context. The User agent's day-in-the-life walkthrough is better positioned than I am to settle that — I'm flagging it, not resolving it.
- **The abuse case I can't size: a coerced/compromised volunteer filing false "deceased" reports at volume.** Step-up (same device, same PIN, same coerced person) does nothing against this — it's a coerced-insider problem, which sits in detection/rate-limiting/corroboration, not auth. I note it so the board doesn't over-read step-up as covering it. No comparable I found addresses coerced-insider casualty falsification via authentication, because authentication structurally can't.
- **Whether "no step-up on creates" quietly becomes wrong if the action surface grows.** I'm reasoning from *today's* verified two-endpoint, create-only surface. If HOS later adds genuinely high-stakes *mutating* volunteer actions (bulk operations, deleting other people's data, changing another volunteer's status), the same rule I'm applying would newly pull those in-scope. The scope should be stated as a **principle** ("step-up attaches to mutation of existing state / change of who-is-acting"), not a frozen list, so it re-derives correctly as the surface changes — this is exactly what OWASP ASVS 2.26 does by delegating to "risk profile" rather than enumerating.

## Confidence Score

**0.72**

Why this high: the core finding is **triangulated across independent, authoritative sources that agree** — banking practice, OWASP ASVS 2.26, the OWASP Authentication Cheat Sheet, OWASP MASWE-0029, and NIST 800-63B all converge on the same narrow trigger (mutation of existing security/recovery/record state, or high-value transaction; not first-time content creation), and NIST independently pins the shared-device/unattended scenario as *the* thing reauth exists for. The recommendation to treat the deceased field as data-quality rather than auth is separately corroborated by the humanitarian + military casualty-recording literature (OHCHR, AR 638-8, ICRC) and by field-tooling practice (ODK/Kobo use confirmation fields, not re-auth). The shared-device attribution framing is directly supported by POS and clinical-device guidance. These are not lone sources.

Why not higher: (1) the two candidates I keep in-scope are both **contingent on facts I can't verify from here** — whether edit/retract will exist, and whether shared-device is the real deployment; if both resolve "no," my scope collapses to essentially "add a confirm dialog," and I want the board to see that honestly rather than have me overstate a step-up regime that may have nothing to attach to. (2) I have **zero HOS-population field data** on whether a confirm-on-deceased is felt as care or friction — that's a User-agent question I'm deferring, not answering. (3) I verified the "create-only, no edit/retract" surface against the proposal's own action-surface note this round, **not** by independently re-grepping the codebase, so I'm inheriting that fact rather than re-establishing it. (4) The coerced-insider abuse case is real, unsized, and explicitly *not* covered by anything in this scope — I'm confident step-up doesn't solve it, less confident about what should. None of these move the direction (small scope, PIN mechanism, deceased-as-data-quality); they cap certainty on how much of the scope is live versus contingent.
