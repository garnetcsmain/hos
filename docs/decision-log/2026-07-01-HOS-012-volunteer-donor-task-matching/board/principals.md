# Principals Review: HOS-2026-012

## My Recommendation
🟡 RESHAPE

The three-option UI mechanic is sound and does not need reshaping. The data model underneath it does. This proposal asks the board to approve "a volunteer skill/needs profile" as if that phrase describes one small, contained thing. It does not. It describes a durable, queryable, cross-time record of what a specific identifiable person can do, what they lack, where they were sent, and when — which is a different and larger object than its name suggests. I will not KILL this, because unlike HOS-2026-010's face path, there is a genuinely minimal version that gets the stated goal without the dangerous part. But the proposal as written does not distinguish the minimal version from the dangerous version, and the board should not sign off on the dangerous one by default.

## Principles Alignment

- **AI recommends, people decide**: ALIGNED, with a caveat that needs stating precisely, not waved through. The proposal correctly does not auto-assign; a human always picks from the three. But this principle was written and has so far been applied to *thing*-recommendations (which offer serves this need). Extending it to *person*-recommendations without comment is a silent scope change I address under Logic Check #2. The mechanism (advisory, non-committing, explainable) is aligned. Whether the mechanism was designed for this object class is a separate question the proposal does not ask itself.

- **Information before interfaces**: NEUTRAL leaning ALIGNED. Three concrete, well-reasoned options is better information than a raw scrollable list, if the scoring is honest. This principle has nothing to say about the privacy question below — it is satisfied by good information design regardless of what's underneath it, which is exactly why it cannot be the principle that clears this proposal. A dashboard can be excellently designed *and* be sitting on top of a database that should not exist. Do not let a green check here stand in for a green check on data minimization.

- **Trust & honesty layer**: CONDITIONALLY ALIGNED. The proposal's own open question #7 already names the exact failure this principle forbids — padding weak or absent matches to always fill three slots — and asks the board to pressure-test it rather than asserting it's handled. That is the right instinct, but it means this principle is not yet satisfied by the proposal as submitted; it is satisfied only if the eventual spec includes an enforced floor (show fewer than three, honestly, when fewer than three real candidates clear a usefulness bar) as a *tested invariant*, not a comment, exactly as HOS-2026-010-D3's condition required for the geofencing flag. I flag this ALIGNED-pending, not ALIGNED.

- **Data minimization / least-PII**: VIOLATED as scoped, ALIGNED if reshaped. This is the center of the review; full reasoning below. The short version: the proposal asks to persist three linkable attributes about a named person — capability, want/lack, and location-correlated task history — indefinitely, in service of a UX feature that does not require indefinite persistence to work. That is the textbook shape of a data-minimization violation: collecting more, and for longer, than the stated goal needs.

- **Auditability**: ALIGNED in spirit, VIOLATED by omission today. The proposal doesn't ask the board to define what gets logged when the AI shows three names to a coordinator, only that the human's eventual choice needs to be attributable (implicit, inherited from the existing Need/Offer claim lifecycle). But "AI recommends, people decide" only produces a real audit trail if the *recommendation* is logged, not only the *decision*. Right now the proposal is silent on this, which by default means it will ship without it — see Logic Check #4.

- **Crisis-grade & reversible**: NEUTRAL. Nothing about a skill/needs profile is harder to run offline or roll back than the existing Need/Offer model; this principle doesn't distinguish the reshaped version from the as-scoped version, so it isn't doing useful work in this specific review. Noted for completeness, not load-bearing.

## Logic Check

**1. Claim: "This is 'AI recommends, people decide' applied to people-to-task matching instead of thing-to-thing matching" (summary, and open_questions framing).**
Does not fully hold. It is true at the mechanism level (advisory scoring, no auto-commit) — I've credited that above. But the claim smuggles in an equivalence between the *object being recommended* and the *thing it's about*, and those are not the same axis. `rankOffersForNeed` recommends which **crate of water** to send; nothing about a crate has interests, can be coerced, or is harmed by being ranked, shown, or not shown. This proposal recommends which **person** to send, and separately, implicitly, invites a coordinator to read a ranked list of volunteers as a list of *who is more useful* right now. A pallet that scores 60 instead of 90 is not diminished by that fact. A volunteer who is never in anyone's top three, silently, run after run, is a fact about that person, visible to whoever operates the system, even if never shown to the volunteer directly. The principle, as originally written, was never tested against "does ranking a person carry a cost that ranking a thing does not," because it never needed to be until now. I am not saying this fails the principle — the "human decides, nothing auto-executes" mechanism genuinely mitigates the *action* risk. I am saying the proposal's claim that this is the *same application* of the principle is not entailed by the evidence; it is a category assumption the board should examine open-eyed rather than accept as already-settled by precedent. This is exactly the gap open_question #board_questions_to_answer (Principals) already flags — good that the proposal asks it, but it should not also assert the answer in its own summary line ("applied to people-to-task matching instead of thing-to-thing" reads as settled, when it's the actual question).

**2. Claim (verified_current_state): "Location-precision-vs-targeting-risk is not a new tension… This proposal's own stated goal… asks for MORE location precision than either precedent allows, for a moving INDIVIDUAL PERSON."**
Holds, and this is the proposal's most honest and most important sentence. I want to be precise about *why* it holds, because the reasoning generalizes beyond location. HOS-2026-007 restricted `Site`/`Need`/`Offer.district` to a coarse string specifically because a *fixed* location (a shelter) that's precise is a raid target. HOS-2026-010 made a *one-time* submission geolocation advisory-only for the same reason. Both precedents concern location as a **snapshot**. This proposal is not asking for a more precise snapshot — re-read `open_questions_for_the_board` item 5 and the `user_impact` paragraph: it wants matching "heavily weighted by location/proximity," for a person who moves, whose skill switches day-to-day, and whose match outcomes (where they were sent, what for) are implied to feed future matching quality. That is not a point, it's a **trajectory**. A trajectory is strictly more identifying than any single point in it, which is exactly the mathematical shape of the finding the board already accepted in HOS-2026-010 about face embeddings (a single derived signal that's individually re-identifying). I make this explicit because the proposal's own framing ("even with zero biometric data involved") reads as if it expects the board to treat that as a mitigating fact. It is not one. The KILL rationale in HOS-2026-010-D1 was never "biometrics are uniquely dangerous data type," it was "a centralized, queryable registry of who is helping, where, is a high-value target for a hostile state actor" — Contrarian's phrase there was "target list for detention/reprisal." A `volunteer_match_history` table with (volunteer_id, district, timestamp, task_category) rows, kept for months, IS that registry, built one honest, well-intentioned matching decision at a time. No face required. The threat model in Venezuela (VenApp, Operación Tun Tun, per HOS-2026-010's own Researcher finding, already accepted board fact) targets *association and movement*, not face geometry specifically — face was simply the most obviously excessive way to build the same dangerous object. This proposal risks building the same object through a side door the board hasn't looked at yet, precisely because nothing about it "sounds like biometrics."

**3. Claim (context.user_impact, "done wrong" clause): the proposal itself names its own worst failure mode ("a matching engine that quietly degrades into 'closest phone GPS ping' and recreates the exact kind of individually-trackable location data this project has twice now worked hard to avoid").**
This is not a claim I need to test — it's the proposal correctly diagnosing itself. I credit this. It means the authors already know the shape of the risk. What the proposal does NOT do is follow that diagnosis to its logical conclusion for the **persistence** question, which is separate from the **precision** question it does address. You can avoid "closest GPS ping" (a precision failure) while still building a dangerous asset through **retention** (a persistence failure) — e.g., district-only location, never raw GPS, matched fresh each time, but every match decision logged forever with volunteer_id, district, and outcome. That system has no GPS ping anywhere in it and is still a movement-pattern registry, just a coarser-grained one, built up over enough data points. Precision and retention are two separate levers; the proposal pulls the first one back (correctly, implicitly) but says nothing about the second.

**4. Claim (implicit, by omission): auditability is satisfied because the human's claim/decision is logged, same as the existing Need/Offer lifecycle.**
Does not hold as stated because it is never stated — I'm surfacing the gap, not refuting an assertion. `rankOffersForNeed` produces a score and `reasons: string[]` per offer, and nothing in `coordination.ts`'s `Need`/`Offer` types persists that scoring output anywhere — only the eventual `claimedByOrgId` state transition is durable. That's correct and sufficient for thing-to-thing matching, because if a claim later turns out to have been a bad call, re-deriving "why did the algorithm suggest this crate" from `category`/`district`/`quantity` at claim-time is cheap and always possible — the inputs are still sitting right there on the Need and Offer rows. Person-to-task matching does not have that property in the same way, because the volunteer's precise availability/skill/location *at the moment of matching* is transient state, not a durable row you can re-query after the fact. If a coordinator sends someone into a bad or dangerous situation based on a bad AI suggestion, "why did the AI suggest these three, specifically" needs to be answerable after the fact for the same reason a black-box triage decision in any other high-stakes system needs to be — but the naive way to make it answerable is to log the full scoring input (skill, need, location) per suggestion-event, which is precisely the durable, timestamped, location-correlated record that Logic Check #2 says is dangerous to keep. This is a real tension the proposal does not surface at all: the auditability principle and the data-minimization principle pull in **opposite directions** on this specific feature, and nobody yet has proposed the reconciliation (my Simplest Solution section below has one).

## Simplest Solution

Occam's razor, applied strictly, separates the proposal into layers it collapsed into one, and treats each with the retention rule the risk actually calls for:

1. **Skill/availability state**: ephemeral. A volunteer's current switchable skill and current personal need ("have car, no gas") should be **current-state fields, overwritten in place, not append-only history**. There is no stated goal this proposal names that requires knowing that a volunteer was a driver last Tuesday — the stated goal is "match tasks to who can do them *right now*." Model it exactly like `Offer.status` (available/committed/depleted) — a mutable current value, not a log. This single design choice removes the "behavioral log" risk almost entirely, because a record that is overwritten, never appended, cannot be replayed as a trajectory. This directly answers the proposal's own open question ("should this be ephemeral/session-scoped") — the answer is: the *value* should be durable-but-mutable (so it survives across days, satisfying "switchable day-to-day" — this is not in tension with persistence, it's in tension with *history*), while the *history of past values* should not be kept at all, by construction, not by policy someone has to remember to enforce.

2. **Match/assignment history**: this is the part that must NOT be durably retained at volunteer-and-location granularity, or must be retained at a granularity too coarse to reconstruct a trajectory. The audit requirement (Logic Check #4) can be satisfied more cheaply than "log everything forever": keep the scoring inputs/reasons attached to the **task/Need record** (which already exists, already has a lifecycle, already has an `orgId` for accountability) rather than attached to a durable per-volunteer record. "Why were these three shown for Need #4821" is answerable from Need #4821's own claim event without needing a `volunteer_history` table that lets someone ask the inverse, dangerous question: "everywhere Volunteer #219 has been sent." Symmetric information, asymmetric danger — the first framing is safe, the second is the registry. If a per-volunteer aggregate is genuinely needed (e.g., "don't re-suggest someone who already declined three tasks today"), that's a same-day, short-TTL counter, not a permanent log — this is the direct analog of HOS-2026-010-R1's "on-device dedup OK with conditions" resolution: keep the minimum aggregate needed for the immediate operational purpose, expire it, and do not retain the raw events that produced it.

3. **Donor as actor type**: this part of the proposal is comparatively low-risk and I'd wave it through with one condition already named correctly in the proposal itself — check the `OrgKind` namespace collision risk (same discipline HOS-2026-011-D1 already applied to "responder") before picking the literal string. A donor reporting "I have a truck, no fuel" is structurally identical to an existing `Offer`/`Need` pair from a non-`Org`-affiliated actor; it doesn't obviously need a new top-level model, just a nullable `orgId` accommodation, which is the same schema shape HOS-2026-011-D2 already accepted for org-optional remote contributors. I'd defer to Expansionist on whether donor/volunteer/beneficiary should unify into one contributor model — that's a scope-and-ambition question, not a principles question, and my razor doesn't have a strong opinion on it as long as whichever shape is chosen doesn't reintroduce the per-person durable history problem above.

**What is lost by simplifying**: a coordinator loses the ability to ask "who has historically been reliable/fast/local" — a real, plausible operational want. I am naming this loss explicitly rather than pretending it's free, per my own directive to separate valuable from consistent. It is a genuinely useful feature to give up. But HOS already made exactly this trade once, at exactly this cost, in HOS-2026-010: the board explicitly declined a face-based system that would have made re-identifying a returning volunteer more convenient, in favor of a system that starts closer to zero on every session, because the convenience wasn't worth the standing risk. This proposal is the same trade wearing different clothes — "remember more about this specific person over time, to match faster/better" — and I don't see an argument in the proposal for why the answer should be different this time, given the population and threat model haven't changed between HOS-2026-010 and HOS-2026-012.

## Technical Debt Created

- **If shipped as scoped (durable profile + durable match history)**: creates a second, later-arriving version of the exact liability HOS-2026-010-D1 was just convened to kill. The debt is not "we'll need to add access controls later" — access controls don't fix a registry that shouldn't exist, they just gate who can query it, and HOS-2026-011-D3/D4 already found this codebase's access-control layer (RLS, org-partitioning) is not built yet even for the data that unambiguously needs it. Building a new sensitive-data surface *while the board's own escalated finding about the existing PII tables' missing `org_id` is still unresolved* compounds a debt that is already sitting on a human's desk, unpaid.
- **If reshaped per my Simplest Solution**: minor, honest debt — a short-TTL dedup counter is a small, well-understood piece of state (cache invalidation, not privacy architecture), and attaching audit reasons to the Need record instead of a volunteer record is arguably *less* new surface than the proposal as scoped, not more. This is the rare case where the safer path is also the cheaper one to build; I flag that explicitly because it undercuts any argument that data-minimization here is a "nice to have we can't afford in a crisis."
- **Either way**: "skill" vs. "capability" naming collision, which HOS-2026-011 already anticipated and named as a condition on this exact proposal. I re-confirm it as live: `coordination.ts` has no `skill` concept today, so there's no code collision yet, but the *conceptual* collision (a task-matching attribute vs. a system-permission attribute, both hanging off the same volunteer) is real and will confuse whoever builds this if the spec doesn't keep them in visibly separate namespaces from day one.

## Confidence Score
0.82

Not higher because: (1) I do not know what "three ranked options" costs in engineering terms if the reshaped, current-state-only, no-history model turns out to score meaningfully worse in practice than a full-history model — I'm asserting the minimal version "still achieves the actual goal" on reasoning from the existing `rankOffersForNeed` precedent (which also has no history and works), not on measured evidence specific to person-matching; that's a Researcher/prototype question, not a pure-logic one. (2) The `OrgKind`/donor namespace question and the skill-vs-capability naming question are real but comparatively low-stakes relative to the retention question, and I have not verified there isn't a third naming collision I haven't thought to check. (3) I'm relying on the HOS-2026-010 judge-decision document's characterization of the Venezuela threat model (VenApp, Operación Tun Tun) rather than re-verifying that reporting independently myself — it was accepted board fact in a prior decision this session treats as settled, which is appropriate, but it means my confidence here is partly inherited, not independently re-derived.

## What I Don't Know
- Whether "district-level, current-state-only, short-TTL-dedup" is actually sufficient for a coordinator's real dispatch workflow, or whether real usage will generate pressure to add "remember this volunteer was good at X" — a pressure that, if it arrives, will arrive framed as a small, reasonable feature request rather than as "let's rebuild the registry we said no to," which is exactly how this kind of scope creep usually re-enters a system. Whoever specs this should pre-decide the answer to that future request now, not when it's asked under time pressure.
- Whether Expansionist's likely proposed unification (volunteer/donor/beneficiary into one contributor model) changes any of my retention reasoning — I don't think it does, since the danger is in the *history*, not the *type name*, but I haven't seen that review to confirm we're not talking past each other.
- The actual UX/decision-science backing (or lack of it) for "exactly three" — I've treated the number itself as principle-neutral (it's a data-minimization non-issue and an honesty issue only insofar as padding-to-three would be dishonest), but I've deferred the "is three the right number at all" question to Researcher/User as the proposal itself does, since it isn't a first-principles question.
