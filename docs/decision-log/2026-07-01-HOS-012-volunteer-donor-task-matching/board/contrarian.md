# Contrarian Review: HOS-2026-012

## My Recommendation
🔴 KILL (the location-weighted person-matching mode as specified) / 🟠 RESHAPE (the rest)

I am not killing "volunteers can express skills" or "donors can exist as an actor type." Those are fine, overdue, boring data-modeling problems. I am killing the one sentence that actually matters in this proposal: *"heavily weighted by location/proximity to maximize response speed"* applied to an individual, moving PERSON. That sentence, if built as written, is the third act of a story this project already told itself twice not to write.

---

## Fatal Flaws Found

### Flaw 1: You are building the tracking system HOS-2026-007 and HOS-2026-010 explicitly refused to build, just one layer removed so it doesn't look like it

**Severity:** 🔴 CRITICAL

**What breaks:** The proposal's own `verified_current_state` section quotes `coordination.ts`'s comment that location is coarse "on purpose — a precise map of where displaced people concentrate is a targeting risk." I read that file. It's real, line 33-34. `Site`, `Need`, and `Offer` all carry a `district: string` — a coarse string, not coordinates, not even a zone-grid (HOS-2026-010's Researcher already confirmed no zone-grid exists anywhere in this repo). That was a deliberate ceiling, not an oversight.

Now look at what this proposal asks for: matching "heavily weighted by location/proximity" for a person, in order to pick the single geographically-closest available body for a task, repeatedly, across every task they're ever assigned. Even if every individual match event only ever displays "district: Petare" to a coordinator, the same volunteer being ranked #1-of-three for tasks in Petare on Monday, Catia on Wednesday, and back in Petare on Friday — timestamped, tied to a stable per-volunteer identity (HOS-2026-010's device-key) — is a movement diary. You don't need raw GPS to reconstruct a pattern. You need repeated, timestamped, identity-linked proximity signals, which is *exactly* what "location-weighted matching, run repeatedly, on a named person" produces by construction. This is not a hypothetical edge case; it is the literal shape of the feature working as designed.

**Why it's real:** This isn't a novel insight I'm inventing — the proposal's own document names the threat model twice (VenApp, Operación Tun Tun) and the Researcher on HOS-2026-010 already established the standard this project is supposed to hold itself to: a "searchable... registry" of who is helping, where, is a "higher-value target... than the missing/found database" specifically because Venezuela is *currently running* an informant-app-and-house-marking campaign against civic participants. A volunteer task-assignment log with location-weighted ranking, subpoenaed or leaked, tells an adversary: this person was near this location at this time, repeatedly, over weeks. That is not a lesser version of the face-registry risk HOS-2026-010-D1 killed — for a moving individual with an ongoing task history, it may be a *richer* signal than a static enrollment photo, because it's temporal and behavioral, not a single snapshot.

**Impact:** If built as specified, this doesn't just repeat a prior mistake — it launders it. A future engineer or auditor asking "does HOS track volunteer location?" gets a technically-true "no, we don't store GPS, only district-level match scores" answer that is functionally false in aggregate. That's worse than the face-biometric proposal in one specific way: the face proposal was honest about what it was (a registry), and got killed on sight. A location-weighted match log *looks* like an advisory scoring feature and could sail through review because no single data point looks alarming.

**Could we fix it?** YES, but only by rejecting the "heavily weighted by proximity, maximize speed" framing as stated, not by adding a mitigation on top of it:
- Coarsen to the same `district` granularity HOS-2026-007 already uses for org matching — same-district vs. different-district as a binary/ordinal signal, not fine-grained proximity ranking within a district.
- Do NOT persist a durable per-volunteer match/assignment history keyed to identity beyond an operational TTL (this is literally what the Principals board question on this same proposal is already asking — I'd escalate it from a question to a hard requirement).
- If genuine sub-district proximity is operationally necessary (and I'm skeptical it is — see Flaw 3), it must be computed ephemerally at request time and discarded, never stored as a queryable log of "volunteer X was near Y at time Z."
- Cost: this is a design constraint, not extra engineering — it's arguably *less* work than building fine-grained geolocation, dedup, and a persistent history store. There is no excuse to build the expensive, riskier version.

### Flaw 2: "Exactly three" is a UX slogan wearing a security-hole costume

**Severity:** 🟠 HIGH

**What breaks:** The proposal wants the AI to surface exactly three ranked candidates for a task. Nobody in this document has asked the obvious adversarial question: if you are always in the top three, you get first crack at every task in your area — which means first crack at supplies, first contact with whoever submitted the need (which could be a beneficiary, a shelter, or a stranded donor), and a plausible, semi-official reason to show up wherever HOS sends you.

Consider the concrete gaming vectors, none of which the proposal addresses:
- A bad actor registers as a volunteer with a broad, generic skill set ("driver," "general labor" — whatever skills correlate with being matchable to the most task categories) and keeps their profile maximally "available" at all times, in a hot district. If proximity is weighted heavily and skill-matching is coarse, this is a low-effort way to dominate the top-3 slot across many tasks — not to help, but to intercept. This is the same "state-actor-adjacent infiltration of a trust system" risk that HOS-2026-010 flagged for identity generally, but here it's worse because it's not just "can this account act" — the system is *actively recommending contact* between this account and vulnerable people/locations, repeatedly, by design.
- Multiple accounts (Sybil) registered by one operator, each tuned to a different skill/district combination, could crowd out three real volunteers for a given task category in a district, effectively DOS-ing legitimate matching without ever touching the account or auth layer. HOS-2026-011 built capability-based authorization; that model has nothing to say about "is this identity trying to game a *ranking* function," because ranking-gaming isn't an authorization violation — you're not doing anything you're not allowed to do, you're just doing it strategically to always be visible.
- Nothing in the proposal defines what "available" means or how it's re-verified. If a volunteer never actively marks themselves unavailable (friction, forgetfulness, bad connectivity), a stale "available" flag becomes a permanent top-3 seat that a coordinator has no way to know is fake without calling every time.

**Why it's real:** This isn't paranoia for its own sake — the entire reason HOS-2026-010 killed face biometrics and HOS-2026-011 exists at all is that this project has already conceded, in writing, that it operates in a threat environment with a motivated, resourced, hostile actor actively trying to identify and intercept civic-response participants. A ranking function that always surfaces the "best" three candidates, with no mention of trust scoring, fraud detection, or anti-gaming design, is a soft target sitting right next to a project that has twice hardened itself against hard targets.

**Impact:** Worst case: a task ("get formula to a shelter," "which stranded driver needs fuel") routes a real donor or beneficiary into contact with an adversarial actor who gamed their way into the top three, in a context where the whole reason the project restricts location precision is that contact with the wrong party is dangerous, not just inconvenient.

**Could we fix it?** Partially. Rate-limiting how often a single volunteer/donor identity can occupy a top-3 slot, requiring active (not just passive/stale) availability confirmation with a short TTL, and layering in *some* trust/vouching signal (even a coarse one — "verified by which org," reusing HOS-2026-011's capability-bundle scoping) would help. But the proposal as written doesn't mention any of this, and "exactly three, weighted by proximity" as the *entire* specification is not a complete design — it's a UX number bolted onto an unspecified trust model. I'd want a real answer to "how does the matcher know an 'available driver' is a real, vetted person and not a fresh, unvetted, conveniently-located account" before green-lighting this at all.

### Flaw 3: The proposal's own success metric guarantees the wasted-trip/real-risk failure mode it says it wants to avoid

**Severity:** 🟠 HIGH

**What breaks:** The stated goal is "maximize response speed" via proximity weighting. But speed and correctness trade off directly against each other the moment "available" and "has the right skill today" are self-reported and switchable "day-to-day" (the proposal's own words). A volunteer who reported themselves as a driver-with-a-car-and-gas at 8am, and is shown as the #1 closest match at 2pm, may by 2pm have: run out of gas (explicitly the proposal's own example need!), switched to cook duty for someone else, lost signal, or simply not checked the app in six hours. None of that invalidates their *proximity* — it invalidates their *usability* — and proximity is the thing being "heavily weighted."

Compare this honestly to `rankOffersForNeed` in `match.ts`: that matcher only ranks offers with `status === "available"`, and org-level offers change status at the pace of an organization's operations (hours to days), not an individual human's minute-to-minute situation. A person's "can I actually do this task right now" state is far more volatile than an org's supply-offer state, and the proposal proposes to weight the *most volatile, least verifiable* signal (a person's current real-world situation) as heavily as the org matcher weights its *most stable, most verifiable* signal (declared district). That's an inversion of the existing system's own risk calculus, not an extension of it.

**Why it's real:** This is not a contrived scenario — it's the literal example the proposal uses to justify the donor concept ("I have a car but no gas"). If a volunteer's own stated need for gas is a first-class scenario this proposal is designed around, then "volunteer with a car" being matched as the #1 closest driver while secretly out of gas is not an edge case, it's the modal case the proposal itself put on the page. In a country with fuel scarcity as a live, named problem, "closest available driver" going stale between the AI's ranking and the human's action is not a tail risk.

**Impact:** A volunteer sent on a bad match doesn't just waste time — this proposal is explicitly about time-critical tasks ("in the next hour"). A volunteer who travels toward a task that's no longer real, or who shows up somewhere expecting backup (fuel, a translator, another set of hands) that a stale match promised and doesn't materialize, can be exposed to exactly the kind of real-world risk (isolated, stranded, in an unfamiliar district, in a country where movement itself can draw unwanted attention) this project claims to care about preventing. The AI recommending a bad option isn't a UX bug here, it's a safety incident.

**Could we fix it?** Partially, with real cost: short TTLs on "available" status that expire it automatically and force re-confirmation before a match is offered; showing the coordinator the *staleness* of each candidate's status as a first-class, un-hideable piece of the three options (not just the score); and — I'd argue — never letting an AI-only signal be the final gate before a real human volunteer physically moves, which the proposal already nominally agrees with ("human decides") but doesn't operationalize into "and here's how the human knows if the AI's information is fresh." This is buildable, but it's more work than the proposal currently scopes, and it directly cuts against "exactly three, ranked by score" if staleness has to visibly compete with proximity for the user's attention.

### Flaw 4: You're not adding one new actor type to an Org-centric schema, you're discovering the schema was never built for actors that aren't orgs, and "donor" is the first of several names about to collide

**Severity:** 🟠 HIGH

**What breaks:** I read `coordination.ts` and the migration SQL directly, not secondhand. Every table that matters — `Site`, `Need`, `Offer` (and their SQL counterparts `sites`, `needs`, `offers`) — has a `NOT NULL REFERENCES orgs(id)` foreign key as its accountable actor. There is no concept anywhere in this schema of an actor that isn't an org row. `OrgKind` is a closed enum (`shelter | responder | ngo | government | hospital | other`) that HOS-2026-011 already found has a real collision on `"responder"` as a person-role name. I checked whether `"donor"` collides the same way: it does not appear anywhere in the current codebase (`coordination.ts`, `match.ts`, or either migration file) as a string literal, enum value, or table name. So the naming collision itself is not repeated — but that's almost worse, because it means the proposal is being let off easy on the wrong question. The right question isn't "does the word 'donor' collide," it's "does bolting a fourth kind of actor onto a model that hard-codes 'the actor is always an org' collide with the schema's actual shape" — and the answer to that is yes, structurally, regardless of naming.

Concretely: if "donor" becomes a new `OrgKind` value, you now have organizations of kind "shelter" and organizations of kind "donor" living in the same table, but a donor per the proposal's own text is explicitly "distinct from the existing Org model" and may be an individual with no organizational affiliation at all. Forcing an individual human into a table whose primary key is literally named `orgs`, whose every column assumes institutional continuity (a `name`, a `kind` implying formal structure), is a category error that will leak into every downstream query, RLS policy, and UI label ("Organización" fields showing a person's name). If instead "donor" becomes a genuinely new top-level table/type (a peer of `Org`), then `Need` and `Offer`'s `orgId: string` foreign keys — hard NOT NULL, singular, referencing exactly one table — cannot express "this need's offering party is a donor, not an org" without a schema migration touching every existing row and every existing query that joins on `orgId`. This is not a clean additive change either way; it's a foundational assumption (every accountable actor is an org) breaking on contact with reality (some accountable actors are individual people), and the proposal treats it as a naming exercise when it's actually a relational-modeling problem.

**Why it's real:** This is exactly the shape of mistake HOS-2026-011 just finished flagging with "responder" — a new person-facing concept assumed to slot cleanly into an existing enum/model that was never designed to hold it. That proposal at least caught its own collision. This proposal's `open_questions_for_the_board` section asks the right question ("Is 'donor' a new OrgKind value, a new top-level actor type... or something else entirely?") but treats it as an open design question rather than the blocking structural issue it is. You cannot spec "add skills, needs, and a donor type" as three independent asks (which is literally how this proposal frames itself) when the third one requires deciding, first, whether HOS has one actor model or two — and that decision changes the shape of the other two.

**Impact:** Ship this without resolving the actor-model question first, and you get a `donorId` column bolted on next to `orgId` as a nullable escape hatch, doubling every downstream conditional ("if orgId else donorId"), doubling RLS policy surface, and creating exactly the kind of quiet, unaudited "which table owns this row" ambiguity that HOS-2026-011 already flagged as a live risk for org-isolation (no route today checks row ownership; PII-adjacent case tables have no `org_id` at all, per that decision's own escalation). Adding a second actor type without resolving this is adding a second way for that already-flagged isolation gap to manifest.

**Could we fix it?** YES, but it requires answering the identity-model question as a prerequisite, not a parallel workstream: either (a) generalize to a single `Actor`/`Participant` concept that both orgs and individuals implement (this is literally what the Expansionist board seat is being asked to evaluate per this proposal's own `board_questions_to_answer` — I'd defer to that seat's ambition but insist the Contrarian's condition is that this decision must be made *before*, not *during*, implementation), or (b) explicitly scope "donor" to mean "a *registered organization* that donates" (i.e., stays inside the existing Org model, individual donors out of scope this cycle) and punt individual, non-affiliated donors to a real future decision, the same way HOS-2026-007 punted "people" entirely rather than half-modeling them. Either is fine. What's not fine is what's currently proposed: defining "donor" as a concept before deciding which of these two worlds it lives in.

## Edge Cases We Haven't Addressed

1. **A volunteer is matched to a task, then immediately re-matched to a second, conflicting task by a second coordinator acting independently** → nothing in the proposal describes any locking/claiming semantics for a *person* the way `Need.status` (open→claimed→received) exists for supply. Two coordinators can send the same human to two places at once and neither will know until the volunteer doesn't show.
2. **A donor with no organizational affiliation and no HOS-2026-010 device-key identity yet** → the proposal assumes donors attach to *something*, but device-key+PIN was scoped to volunteers. Is a donor authenticated at all, or is this a repeat of the anonymous-submission trust question HOS-2026-010 already wrestled with, for a *new* actor type, without reusing that decision's reasoning?
3. **A volunteer's skill/need profile after they stop volunteering** → skills are "switchable day to day," implying an ongoing, live profile. What's the retention/deletion story when someone stops participating? HOS-2026-010-D2's conditions required a consent/retention policy for a much thinner data footprint (name, org, role, device key); this proposal adds an ongoing behavioral history on top and doesn't mention retention at all.
4. **A viral surge (this project's own recurring stress case, per HOS-2026-002-D4 precedent cited in this very proposal)** → what happens to "exactly three, location-weighted" when 200 new volunteers self-register in one district in one hour? Does the ranking degrade gracefully, or does the top-3 slot become winner-take-all for whoever registered first/fastest, which is its own gaming vector under load.
5. **A hostile actor submits a fabricated "donor" need to lure a real volunteer to a physical location** → the proposal's donor concept lets anyone claiming to be a donor request "volunteer help" for a logistics issue. Combined with location-weighted matching pulling in the *closest* real volunteer, this is a plausible entrapment vector that doesn't require compromising any HOS account — just submitting a request.

## Questions for the Proposer

1. Walk me through, concretely, what data a subpoena or breach of the match/assignment log would expose about a single named volunteer over a two-week period. If your honest answer is "their approximate district-level movement pattern over time," you've built the tracking system HOS-2026-010 killed, just with an extra layer of indirection.
2. What stops one identity (or a small cluster of coordinated identities) from tuning their profile to dominate the top-3 slot across many tasks in a district? If the answer is "nothing yet," this cannot ship without that answer first.
3. Is "donor" meant to include unaffiliated individuals, or only registered organizations that give things away? Answer this before writing a single line of schema — it changes whether this is an `OrgKind` addition or a new top-level actor/relational model.
4. Where did "exactly three" come from? If it's a guess, say so, and let the Researcher's review actually test it rather than treating it as settled.
5. What is the TTL on "available," and what happens to a match already shown to a coordinator when the underlying volunteer's status changes before the coordinator acts? Silent staleness or a visible, honest "this may be out of date" flag?

## What Would Change My Mind?

If you could show me:
- A concrete design where sub-district proximity signal is computed and used ephemerally (at match time) and never persisted as a durable, queryable, identity-linked history — i.e., the system can answer "who's free and close right now" without being able to answer "where has volunteer X been for the last month"
- A real anti-gaming mechanism for the top-3 slot (rate limits, trust/vouching tied to HOS-2026-011's capability model, mandatory active re-confirmation of availability) specified up front, not as a future patch
- A resolved answer — before implementation, not during — to whether HOS has one actor model or two, with "donor" placed explicitly on one side of that line
- Evidence (even anecdotal, from the Researcher's crisis-response precedent search) that "exactly three" beats "ranked list of N" or "top match + honest count of runners-up" in an actual field context, not just a UX intuition

...then I'd raise my confidence from 20% (that this ships safely as currently specified) to something worth a genuine RESHAPE-with-conditions rather than a KILL on the matching-mode centerpiece.

## Confidence Score
**0.82** — I'm 82% confident the location-weighted, exactly-three matching mode as specified recreates a materially significant version of the exact risk this project already spent two decisions avoiding, and that the donor-actor-type question is structurally unresolved, not just under-specified.

Why not higher? I haven't seen the Researcher's precedent search on crisis-response volunteer dispatch (CrisisCleanup, Team Rubicon, VOAD) — it's possible there's a real, validated pattern for bounded-precision proximity matching in exactly this context that de-risks more of this than I'm giving credit for. And I haven't seen the Expansionist's unified-actor-model proposal, which might resolve Flaw 4 more elegantly than I'm assuming from a KILL-first posture.

## Final Note

This project has now, twice, in the same afternoon of decisions, looked at "more location precision would help us move faster" and correctly said no because the population being helped operates under a hostile state actor actively hunting exactly this kind of signal. This proposal asks the same question a third time, about a population (individual volunteers with ongoing task histories) that is arguably *more* exposed than either prior case, not less — and the proposal's own text names the same threat actors (VenApp, Operación Tun Tun) that killed the face-biometric path. The "AI recommends, people decide" framing is doing a lot of work to make this feel safe by association with a principle this project genuinely holds — but a human clicking "assign" on a location-weighted top-3 doesn't make the underlying data trail any less reconstructable. Fix the location-weighting and the actor-model question before this goes anywhere near a build ticket; the skills and needs concepts are fine and can proceed on their own merits once they're not welded to the risky part.
