# Researcher Review: HOS-2026-012

*Volunteer skill/needs model, a new "donor" actor type, and AI-assisted person-to-task matching ("exactly three ranked options, human decides")*
Reviewer: Researcher Agent · Date: 2026-07-01 · Method: live web research (37 sources across four parallel threads + direct primary-source verification) + read-only codebase survey (`apps/web/app/lib/domain/coordination.ts`, `apps/web/app/lib/coordination/match.ts`) + cross-check against this project's own HOS-2026-007, HOS-2026-010, and HOS-2026-011 board records.

## My Recommendation

**RESHAPE.** Two of this proposal's three data-model asks (donor actor type; location-weighted person-to-task matching, at coarse granularity) have real, reasonably strong precedent and should proceed with specific structural corrections below. The third — **"switchable day-to-day skill"** — has **no supporting precedent anywhere I could find**, and the strongest, most-documented real-world systems (Team Rubicon, ESAR-VHP/MRC) actively **contradict** it: every mature disaster-volunteer system treats skill as a **persistent, cumulative, verified record you select from per deployment**, never a record you reset or toggle per day. That's not a nitpick; it's the load-bearing design choice the proposal's own framing example ("a driver one day, a cook the next") rests on, and it appears to be untested against any comparable rather than modeled on one. Separately, **"exactly three"** has no real UX evidence behind it either — worse, the one body of research the proposal implicitly leans on (choice-overload) has a landmark 2010 meta-analysis (Scheibehenne et al., 50 studies) finding the average effect of "more choices" on satisfaction is **essentially zero**, and the field's own consensus is that the original jam study is a fragile, highly-moderated effect, not a robust law. Real crisis-triage practice (START, the actual most-deployed US mass-casualty protocol) does the opposite of "three ranked candidates" — it uses **4 fixed categorical buckets** decided by a 15-second deterministic rule, not a ranked shortlist at all. "Three" is an arbitrary number the board should either justify from HOS's own future usage data or drop in favor of "as many genuinely-qualifying candidates as clear a floor, capped at a small number for screen real estate" — which is a different, more honest design than "always exactly three."

On the privacy question this family of decisions has trained the board to scrutinize hardest: the proposal's own framing is basically right, and the underlying literature makes it *sharper*, not looser. A district-level, sparse task-assignment log is not equivalent to the biometric/raw-GPS harms of HOS-2026-010 — but it is not "safe by virtue of being coarse" either. De Montjoye et al.'s peer-reviewed re-identification result (the closest real study to this exact question) explicitly tested coarsening as a mitigation and found identifiability decays only as roughly the *tenth root* of resolution — i.e., coarsening is a weak, slow-acting defense, not an on/off switch. And Singapore's TraceTogether — a purpose-built, coarse, non-biometric, non-GPS *proximity/association log*, publicly promised to be used only for contact tracing — was compelled into a murder investigation and is now documented as the canonical "we said we'd never use it for X, then we did" case. That is precisely the trust-erosion mechanism a task-assignment log with no independent policy backstop would recreate, at a smaller but non-zero scale, for a system serving Venezuelan volunteers under an active state doxxing campaign (Operación Tun Tun, now with a confirmed *named victim* — see below).

## Comparable Systems

- **Team Rubicon (persistent skill profile, zone-level matching):** Volunteers ("Greyshirts") complete a "skills and experiences" section of their Roll Call profile *once*; deployment selection draws from that accumulating, stored record, built on Microsoft Dynamics 365's Common Data Model for Nonprofits. CIO-level framing: "it's easy for us to continually build up the story of each individual" — a durable record, not a daily reset. On location: COO Art delaCruz on record explicitly describing **zone/territory-level** control ("We made it our obligation not to deploy people from a known spread zone to a non-spread zone... we've used our system to restrict movement in that fashion") across ~25 defined territories, not GPS coordinates. → **Outcome: contradicts "switchable skill"; supports coarse-zone (not GPS) location-weighting.** ([Microsoft Dynamics 365 blog](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/dynamics-365/blog/business-leader/2020/07/14/team-rubicon-refining-the-logistics-of-disaster-response/); [Microsoft customer story](https://www.microsoft.com/en/customers/story/1523704728275894394-team-rubicon-nonprofit-dynamics-365); [diginomica](https://diginomica.com/ngo-team-rubicon-partners-microsoft-move-reactive-preventive-disaster-zone-response); [Roll Call](https://rollcall.teamrubiconusa.org/))
- **ESAR-VHP / Medical Reserve Corps (the single strongest documented "skills as persistent record" precedent):** The federal Emergency System for Advance Registration of Volunteer Health Professionals maintains standardized state registries recording licensure, credentials, training history, and skills, tiered into four categories (Administrative/Leadership → Trained Position-Assigned → Oriented No-Position → Registered-only). Registration happens **in advance of a disaster**, is verified, and persists. → **Outcome: the most rigorous available precedent, and it is the polar opposite of "switchable day-to-day."** ([ASPR ESAR-VHP](https://aspr.hhs.gov/ESAR-VHP/Pages/default.aspx); [Wikipedia: ESAR-VHP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESAR-VHP))
- **FEMA Volunteer Reception Center (VRC) model:** the sector-standard mechanism for *unaffiliated/spontaneous* volunteers (the population closest to HOS's actual volunteers) is a **one-time controlled intake**: register, state skills/limitations, get safety orientation, get referred to a task. This is a single registration event feeding an assignment process, not a repeatable daily-switch mechanic. I could not retrieve FEMA's actual intake form field-by-field (fema.gov and emilms.fema.gov blocked automated fetches with 403s across multiple attempts) — flagging this as an access limitation, not a confirmed absence of detail. → **Outcome: structurally implies persistent one-time capture; weak/circumstantial evidence against a switchable-skill model, not a strong contradiction because I couldn't read the actual form.** ([FEMA "Managing Spontaneous Volunteers"](https://www.fema.gov/pdf/donations/ManagingSpontaneousVolunteers.pdf) — fetch blocked; [FEMA Volunteer and Donations Management Support Annex](https://www.fema.gov/sites/default/files/2020-07/fema_nrf_support-annex_volunteer.pdf) — fetch blocked)
- **National VOAD:** a coordinating body (the "4 Cs" — cooperation, communication, coordination, collaboration) operating through state VOADs/local COADs. **No published unified volunteer-skills data model exists from NVOAD itself** — this is a genuine gap in public precedent, not a retrieval failure; their materials describe organizational coordination doctrine, not IT architecture. → **Outcome: silent, honestly.** ([NVOAD](https://www.nvoad.org/); [NVOAD Disaster Case Management Guidelines](https://www.nvoad.org/wp-content/uploads/dcmguidelines-final.pdf))
- **Digital Humanitarian Network / MicroMappers / crisis-mapping microtasking:** the closest precedent to "AI-assisted task assignment to distributed volunteers" is real, but it's a **different mechanic** than HOS-012 proposes — it's remote *digital* volunteers processing a queue of small classification/tagging microtasks (image tagging, geolocation-of-a-report), not physical field volunteers being matched to physical logistics tasks by proximity. HOS-2026-011's own board record (which I re-checked) already independently reached the same conclusion for the *authorization* axis: SBTF/MicroMappers organize by **task function**, not geography — worth noting because that finding, from a sibling proposal in this exact family, corroborates the same structural lesson from an independent angle. → **Outcome: real but distal precedent — supports "task-function-based assignment" as a concept, does not validate location-weighted physical dispatch specifically.** ([iRevolutions: Digital Humanitarian Response](https://irevolutions.org/2013/01/20/digital-humanitarian-micro-tasking/); [Digital Humanitarian Network — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Humanitarian_Network))
- **CrisisCleanup:** the sector's leading multi-org disaster work-order platform (born out of Superstorm Sandy). Its documented design principle is **"collaborative accountability," not command-and-control** — orgs claim worksites they have capacity for; the platform's job is visibility (who's working where) so orgs don't duplicate effort, not an algorithm assigning a specific volunteer to a specific task. I found no public technical documentation of a proximity-weighted, ranked, few-candidates matching engine inside CrisisCleanup — its "matching" is closer to a shared claimable map/list. → **Outcome: real, well-known precedent, but for a different matching philosophy (visibility + self-selection by orgs) than HOS-012's AI-ranks-three-people-for-a-coordinator-to-pick model.** ([CrisisCleanup About](https://crisiscleanup.org/about); [Technical.ly writeup](https://technical.ly/civic-news/crisis-cleanup/))
- **Ride-share/gig dispatch (proximity-scoring mechanics only, explicitly not the labor model):** confirms proximity-weighted, ranked-candidate matching is a mature, well-understood mechanic — but the "three" that appears in this literature is **structurally different** from what's proposed. It refers to **non-exclusive dispatch**: broadcasting a *request* to 3 drivers simultaneously who compete/decline in sequence (an automated, adversarial multi-offer race), not "show a human three ranked options and they pick one." Citing ride-share precedent for "three" specifically would be citing the wrong mechanic. → **Outcome: supports proximity-weighted ranking as a general pattern; does NOT support "exactly three shown to a human decider" as a validated number — that's a different design.** ([Non-Exclusive Notifications for Ride-Hailing at Lyft, arXiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2603.21533))
- **Prehospital trauma triage clinical decision support (OSISP model) — the one real "top three shown to a human under time pressure" precedent I found:** a clinical AI system filtering "top three" predictors for/against a diagnosis to a human clinician. I fetched the primary source directly: it does **not** state an evidence-based rationale for the number three — it justifies limiting information generally ("excessive information...may cause irritation," avoiding "increased cognitive load") but never derives or tests "three" specifically, and cites no choice-overload/decision-fatigue literature at all. → **Outcome: the closest real analog to "three ranked items under stress" is a design choice with an *unstated* number, not a validated one — this actively undercuts treating "three" as evidence-based anywhere in this space.** ([OSISP model, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12701220/))
- **START triage (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) — the actual dominant US mass-casualty protocol:** uses **4 fixed color-coded categories** (black/red/yellow/green) via a ~15-second deterministic assess-and-tag rule with no ranking and no "top N" concept at all. This is the field's real answer to "how do you make a fast, defensible decision under crisis time pressure with minimal training," and its answer is categorical triage, not a ranked shortlist. → **Outcome: the sector's actual fast-decision doctrine looks nothing like "exactly three ranked options" — worth the board treating as a structurally different, better-precedented alternative pattern.** ([StatPearls: EMS Mass Casualty Triage](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK459369/); [REMM: START Adult Triage](https://remm.hhs.gov/startalgotext.htm))
- **Salesforce Nonprofit Success Pack (NPSP) — the strongest domain-specific donor/org structural precedent:** used by thousands of nonprofits/NGOs specifically for this exact problem. Structure: **`Contact`** always represents an individual (donor or otherwise); **`Account`** represents an organization *or* a system-generated **"Household Account"** for an individual/family with no formal org. Every `Contact` has a required `AccountId` — individuals-without-an-org get auto-assigned a Household Account so the schema stays uniform (one relationship shape for all contacts, org or not). → **Outcome: real, on-point precedent for "two linked types, individual always gets a synthetic parent" as the pattern for a donor/org split** — directly answers HOS-012's `orgId`-currently-required problem. ([Salesforce Data Model Gallery — NPSP](https://developer.salesforce.com/docs/platform/data-models/guide/nonprofit-success-pack.html); [Trailhead — NPSP data model](https://trailhead.salesforce.com/content/learn/modules/nonprofit-success-pack-administration-basics/understand-the-npsp-data-model))
- **Stripe (the general two-sided-marketplace precedent, verified directly against API docs):** uses the *opposite* pattern from NPSP — **one polymorphic `Customer`/`Account` type** with `business_name` and `individual_name` as separate nullable fields on the same object (discriminator-by-presence), and in the newer Accounts v2 API an explicit `business_type: individual` discriminator with conditional nested fields. → **Outcome: real, on-point precedent for the alternative structural pattern — "one actor type + discriminator field" — which the board should weigh against NPSP's "two linked types."** ([Stripe Customer object](https://docs.stripe.com/api/customers/object); [Stripe Accounts v2](https://docs.stripe.com/accounts-v2/use-accounts-as-customers))
- **FEMA / National VOAD — "unaffiliated donations" as a named, separately-coordinated category:** the Volunteer and Donations Management Support Annex (ESF #6) is real and treats **unaffiliated/spontaneous volunteers** and **unsolicited/spontaneous donations** as two distinct, named problem categories, each with its own coordination mechanism (Volunteer Reception Centers for people; separate donations-coordination structures for goods) — precisely because affiliated-vs-unaffiliated changes what verification/trust workflow applies. This is real doctrinal precedent for the *need* for a structurally distinct "unaffiliated individual" category; it does not prescribe a schema. → **Outcome: strong conceptual support for "donor" as genuinely structurally distinct, not just a data-entry convenience.** (Direct PDF fetch blocked by fema.gov bot protection on repeated attempts; corroborated via multiple independent secondary sources describing the annex's contents, e.g. [ICPTTA summary](https://nmvvrc.org/media/u3emt05e/volunteer-and-donations-management_fema.ppt), FEMA's [Managing Spontaneous Volunteers guide](https://www.fema.gov/pdf/donations/ManagingSpontaneousVolunteers.pdf))
- **UNHCR / Rohingya + Afghanistan 2021 (carried forward from HOS-2026-010, re-cited because this proposal's own text invokes it):** still the governing precedent for "a captured registry becomes the adversary's target list." Nothing in this proposal's design changes that risk category — it lowers the *sensitivity* of the data (no biometrics) but does not zero it out, per the de Montjoye/TraceTogether findings below. → **Outcome: the standard this proposal must be held to, not a new finding, but worth restating because the proposal's own risk framing correctly invokes it.**

## Ground-Truth Data

- **Re-identification from coarse location data — the single most load-bearing number for the privacy question:** de Montjoye, Hidalgo, Verleysen & Blondel, *"Unique in the Crowd: The Privacy Bounds of Human Mobility"* (Scientific Reports, 2013; 1.5M people, 15 months of mobile-phone antenna-level data). **Four spatio-temporal points are enough to uniquely re-identify 95% of individuals.** Critically, the paper explicitly tests *coarsening* (lowering spatial/temporal resolution) as a mitigation and finds uniqueness decays only as roughly the **1/10 power** of resolution — i.e., coarsening is a real but very slow-acting defense, not a binary safe/unsafe switch. (Scientific Reports, peer-reviewed) → Applied to HOS: this paper's own base case (hourly, antenna-level) is finer-grained than "sparse, district-level, occasional dispatch events," so it does not directly prove a task-assignment log is equally re-identifying — but its central finding (coarsening helps slowly, not categorically) directly contradicts any assumption that "we used district, not GPS" makes an assignment log safe by construction. [Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep01376); [MIT DSpace full text](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/88233/de%20Montjoye-2013-Unique%20in%20the%20crowd.pdf)
- **Choice-overload literature is contested, not settled — directly undercuts "exactly three" as evidence-based:** Iyengar & Lepper's 2000 jam study (24 vs. 6 flavors; famous 3%-vs-30%-purchase finding) is the origin of the "fewer choices sell/satisfy better" folk-claim underlying this proposal's framing. But Scheibehenne, Greifeneder & Todd's 2010 meta-analysis of **50 studies** found the **average effect size across the literature is statistically indistinguishable from zero**, with results going both directions depending on moderators (existing preferences, category expertise, etc.). → This is not a fringe rebuttal; it is the field's standard citation for "choice overload is real only under specific, moderated conditions, not a general law you can invoke to justify a specific number." [Re-Analyzing a Meta-Analysis (ResearchGate)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/256846155_Re-Analyzing_a_Meta-Analysis_Another_Look_at_Choice_Overload); [Atticus Li summary](https://atticusli.com/replication-crisis/choice-overload-jam-study/)
- **Miller's "7±2" is separately, specifically flagged by its own author as misapplied to exactly this kind of design decision:** Miller himself said the "magical number seven" was about discrimination of unidimensional sensory stimuli (pitch, loudness), not "how many options should a UI show." UX literature treats "limit choices to 7 (or any fixed N) because of Miller's Law" as a well-known myth. If the board or a future implementer reaches for "three is safely under 7±2" as backup reasoning, that reasoning is specifically invalid per the source material itself. [UX Myths — Myth #23](https://uxmyths.com/post/931925744/myth-23-choices-should-always-be-limited-to-seven); [Laws of UX — Miller's Law](https://lawsofux.com/millers-law/)
- **Singapore TraceTogether — the closest real "coarse non-biometric log repurposed by the state" precedent:** a purpose-built, coarse (proximity, not GPS), non-biometric contact log, publicly promised for epidemiological use only. In January 2021 the government confirmed police could compel its production under the Criminal Procedure Code, and disclosed it had *already* been used in a murder investigation — the promise had already been broken before the admission. [MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2021/01/05/1015734/singapore-contact-tracing-police-data-covid/); [MIT Technology Review — "Broken promises"](https://www.technologyreview.com/2021/01/11/1016004/singapore-tracetogether-contact-tracing-police/); [NPR](https://www.npr.org/sections/coronavirus-live-updates/2021/01/05/953604553/singapore-says-covid-19-contact-tracing-data-can-be-requested-by-police)
- **Iran SIAM spyware, 2022 protests — metadata correlation, no GPS or biometrics, used to target medics/aid providers:** leaked government documents (The Intercept) show Iran's Communications Regulatory Authority correlating which phone numbers connected to which cell towers (CDR-style metadata) to identify protest attendees, explicitly including medics and aid providers — a close structural analog to logistics/task volunteers. [The Intercept](https://theintercept.com/2022/10/28/iran-protests-phone-surveillance/)
- **ZunZuneo (USAID "Cuban Twitter," 2010–2012):** built an actionable political profile (inferred "political tendencies," demographics) on ~60,000+ subscribers from **usage metadata alone** — no GPS, no biometrics. Confirms metadata/association data, not just biometric or raw-location data, is sufficient to build a targetable profile. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZunZuneo); [AP via CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-secretly-built-cuban-twitter-but-why/)
- **Venezuela — Operación Tun Tun now has a confirmed, named fatal case directly on the "civic-participation volunteer" analogy this board already drew in HOS-2026-010:** Jesús Manuel Martínez Medina, an electoral witness for Vente Venezuela, was detained after the July 2024 election and died in custody in November 2024. A UN fact-finding mission found detentions in this pattern originated from reports made through **VenApp** by pro-government individuals. This corroborates — with a named victim — the exact mechanism (informant app → identification → detention) HOS-2026-010's Researcher review already flagged as the governing threat model, and it is the closest documented real-world case to "a task-adjacent civic-participation record becoming the input to a detention." [Operation Tun Tun — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Tun_Tun); [PROVEA](https://provea.org/opinion/operacion-tun-tun-el-delivery-del-terrorismo-de-estado/)
- **ICRC/Privacy International — the sector's own flagship document treats metadata as a serious, comparable risk category, not a lesser one:** *"The Humanitarian Metadata Problem: Doing No Harm in the Digital Era"* (2018) explicitly frames association/activity metadata (not just content or biometrics) as capable of revealing "sleep patterns, travel routines, frequent contacts" with real risk in conflict environments. I could not get clean full-text extraction of this PDF to pull exact wording — flagging this as lower-confidence sourcing pending direct document review by board staff, but the framing itself is corroborated by multiple independent secondary descriptions of the report. [ICRC/PI PDF](https://www.icrc.org/sites/default/files/document/file_list/the_humanitarian_metadata_problem_-_icrc_and_privacy_international.pdf)

## Assumption Tests

| Assumption | Verdict | Evidence |
|---|---|---|

No comparable models "switchable skill" as the proposal frames it. The board should treat this as a genuinely novel design choice, not a codification of existing practice, and pressure-test it directly rather than assume precedent backs it. |
| Location-weighted matching should be precise/GPS to "maximize speed" | **PARTIALLY CONTRADICTED** | Every system with documented location-matching granularity (Team Rubicon's ~25 territories/zones) operates at **coarse zone/regional level**, not GPS — and this is presented as a deliberate operational choice (containing spread-zone movement), not a limitation they'd remove if they could. Combined with this project's own precedent (HOS-2026-007's `district` field, HOS-2026-010's advisory-only geo), the sector consensus and this project's own prior decisions point the same direction: coarse, not precise. |
| "Exactly three" is a validated number for high-stress/time-pressured decision UX | **CONTRADICTED** | The choice-overload literature's own field-standard meta-analysis (Scheibehenne et al. 2010, 50 studies) found an average effect indistinguishable from zero; Miller's 7±2 is explicitly disclaimed by its own author as inapplicable to this kind of design choice; the one real "top-N under time pressure" clinical precedent I found (OSISP trauma triage) uses "three" with **no stated evidence-based rationale**; and the sector's actual fastest, most-deployed crisis-decision protocol (START) uses categorical triage, not a ranked shortlist, at all. "Three" reads as an arbitrary starting number, not evidence. |
| A coarse, non-biometric task-assignment log carries materially lower risk than HOS-2026-010's biometric registry | **SUPPORTED, but with an important caveat — lower, not low** | Genuinely true that this is a smaller-magnitude risk than a face/biometric registry (no permanently unforgeable identifier, no continuous GPS). But de Montjoye's coarsening result (identifiability decays slowly, not categorically, with resolution) and the TraceTogether precedent (a coarse, non-biometric, purpose-limited log still got compelled into unrelated state use) both show "coarse and non-biometric" is not the same as "safe," especially absent an explicit policy/legal backstop against repurposing — which this proposal does not yet specify. |
| "Donor" needs to be a structurally distinct actor type, not shoehorned into `Org` | **SUPPORTED** | Real, decades-old sector doctrine (FEMA/VOAD's "unaffiliated/spontaneous" donor and volunteer categories) treats affiliated-vs-unaffiliated as a first-order distinction specifically because it changes the verification/trust workflow — directly mirroring HOS's own current structural gap (every need/offer traces to an `orgId`; there is no path for a person with no org). Two real, live technical patterns exist to implement this (Salesforce NPSP's "two linked types, individual gets a synthetic parent" vs. Stripe's "one type + discriminator field") — the board should pick one deliberately rather than treat this as unprecedented territory. |

This proposal's new actor-type/skill vocabulary must be checked against that exact collision before any implementation, not treated as a future concern. |

## Unknown Unknowns

- **Whether "switchable skill" solves a real HOS-specific problem invisible to me.** Every comparable I found assumes a *pool* of volunteers large enough that "today's cook" and "today's driver" are different people, not the same person context-switching. If HOS's actual volunteer pool is small enough that the same 20 people rotate roles daily out of necessity, "switchable" might be a genuine, HOS-specific innovation rather than an unprecedented risk — but I have no HOS-specific volunteer-pool-size data to test that against, and neither, as far as I can tell, does the proposal.
- **What HOS's actual future usage data would show about "three."** My finding is that three is *unvalidated*, not that it's *wrong*. A/B data from HOS's own coordinators (do they act faster/more confidently on 3 vs. 5 vs. "as many as clear a quality floor"?) would settle this far better than any external literature — none of which was generated in a Spanish-language, low-end-device, active-crisis, low-literacy context resembling HOS's actual users. I flag this as the single most answerable-by-piloting unknown in this review.
- **The magnitude of re-identification risk at HOS's actual sparsity, which no paper I found tests directly.** De Montjoye's base case is hourly/antenna-level — finer than "occasional dispatch events, district-level, over weeks/months." I can say with confidence that coarsening is a *weak*, not *absent*, defense; I cannot give the board a number for "how many task-assignment records, at what sparsity, before a specific volunteer's pattern becomes reconstructable" — that number does not exist in the literature at HOS's specific resolution, and estimating it would be a guess dressed as research.
- **Whether NPSP's or Stripe's structural pattern is actually the better fit for HOS's specific need/offer graph**, versus some third option neither precedent tested (e.g., a lightweight "unaffiliated org" sentinel row rather than a truly separate type). I've surfaced the two dominant real-world patterns; picking between them for HOS's specific schema is a design decision, not a research question, and I'd defer to whoever owns the domain model.
- **Whether there exists real precedent for "logistics/task volunteers" specifically (not protesters, electoral witnesses, journalists, or medics) being targeted via an assignment-style log in an authoritarian context.** I looked hard for this and did not find it. The Tun Tun/electoral-witness case and Iran's medic-targeting are the closest analogs, both civic-participation roles structurally similar to a task volunteer, but neither is the exact case. This is a real gap in the evidence, not a reassurance — the absence of a documented incident for this precise role type is different from evidence that the risk doesn't apply to it.

## Confidence Score

**0.74**

Why this high: the two most decision-critical findings are each triangulated across independent, authoritative sources rather than resting on one citation. "Switchable skill has no precedent" is corroborated across three independent systems (Team Rubicon's own executives on record, the federal ESAR-VHP registry design, and FEMA's VRC one-time-intake model) that all point the same direction without contradiction. "Exactly three is unvalidated" is corroborated by a landmark meta-analysis (Scheibehenne, 50 studies), Miller's own disavowal of his law's misuse, a directly-fetched primary source (OSISP) that uses "three" with no stated rationale, and the sector's actual fastest real triage doctrine (START) using a structurally different pattern entirely. The privacy findings (de Montjoye + TraceTogether + Iran SIAM + ZunZuneo) triangulate the "coarse/non-biometric is lower-risk-but-not-safe" conclusion from genuinely independent domains (academic re-identification theory, a real repurposing scandal, and two real metadata-targeting incidents).

Why not higher: (1) several primary FEMA source documents were bot-blocked (403) across repeated attempts by two independent research threads — I'm relying on secondary corroboration for FEMA/VOAD specifics rather than the primary annex text itself, and flagged this everywhere it applies rather than smoothing over it. (2) De Montjoye's paper doesn't test a resolution as sparse as HOS's actual proposed use, so my privacy conclusion ("weak defense, not zero risk") is directionally solid but I can't hand the board a precise re-identification probability at HOS's specific sparsity — that number doesn't exist yet. (3) I could not find a documented targeting incident for the specific role type ("logistics/task volunteer," as opposed to protester/electoral-witness/medic) — a real gap I'm flagging rather than papering over with analogy. (4) The ICRC/Privacy International metadata-report content is corroborated only via secondary summaries; I'd want board staff to pull the primary PDF directly before treating exact wording as settled. None of these weaken the two headline findings (switchable-skill unprecedented; "three" unvalidated); they cap my certainty on the precise magnitudes of the privacy risk and on a couple of secondary-source-only citations.
