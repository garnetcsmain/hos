# Researcher Review: HOS-2026-008-R1 (addendum, incl. role-scoping)

*On-device-only face-uniqueness dedup at registration + role/action-scoped advisory geolocation*
Reviewer: Researcher Agent · Date: 2026-07-01 · Method: live web research (this round specifically re-verified the two questions an earlier interim pass could not — see provenance note below) + re-read of `board/researcher.md` (original) and `board/r1-interim-orchestrator-analysis.md`

> **Provenance note.** The interim orchestrator analysis (`r1-interim-orchestrator-analysis.md`) explicitly flagged two claims it could **not** verify because WebSearch was down that round: (1) whether leaked face *embeddings* (not photos) are reversible/fingerprint-like, and (2) whether real crowdsourced crisis platforms distinguish remote/digital from field/physical contributors. This review verifies both with live sources. Both were open questions; both now have answers, and one of them changes the risk calculus in a way that matters.

## My Recommendation

**RESHAPE** — same direction as the original board (do not build a server-side biometric registry; device-key+PIN for login is correct), and the on-device-only refinement is a **genuine, structural improvement** over the killed design, not cosmetic. I can now confirm the single fact the interim pass had to leave open: **"we only store the embedding, not the photo" is a materially weaker privacy claim than it sounds** — a *raw* face embedding is reconstructable back into a recognizable face at 75–95% success with published 2025 tooling. That does **not** flip this to KILL, because the on-device design's core mitigation (nothing is transmitted or server-stored) means there is no central embedding corpus to leak in the first place. But it means the residual local-cache exposure must be treated as real biometric data — invertible and unrotatable — and hardened accordingly (cancelable-template transform, backup exclusion, shared-device wipe), not waved off as "just numbers." The role-scoping refinement is **well-supported by real prior art** and I recommend the interim note's sharpening of it: **scope the geo check to the submission/action, not a persistent account role.** Real humanitarian platforms already work this way.

Three verdicts up front, since this round exists to settle them:
- **Q1 — Are leaked embeddings reversible/fingerprint-like?** → **YES, for raw embeddings** (SUPPORTED, high confidence). Reversible enough to reconstruct a recognizable, re-identifiable face; and treated as biometric personal data by regulators regardless of pseudonymization. The "it's only an embedding" defense is weaker than commonly assumed. Mitigable *only* by a cancelable/salted transform, which the current design does not yet specify.
- **Q2 — Do real crisis platforms distinguish remote/digital from field contributors?** → **YES** (SUPPORTED, high confidence). The entire Digital Humanitarian Network / Standby Task Force / MicroMappers model is remote-first digital volunteers who are never expected to be physically present; Ushahidi trusts by corroboration, not identity; Translators Without Borders (100k+ remote volunteers) self-register with no local voucher. The principal's instinct is not a corner case — it is the dominant pattern.
- **Q3 — Does on-device-only biometric *uniqueness/dedup* exist in production, and how is local persistence handled?** → **PARTIALLY** (mixed). On-device face *verification* (1:1, is-this-the-same-person) ships in production from multiple vendors. But on-device *uniqueness/dedup* (1:N, has-this-person-enrolled-before) fundamentally needs a shared comparison surface, which is exactly why the flagship proof-of-personhood system (World ID) went **server-side**, not local. A local-only cache can structurally only catch same-device reuse — which the design already concedes — and even that guarantee is soft, because IndexedDB is best-effort/evictable/user-clearable.

## Comparable Systems

- **Digital Humanitarian Network / Standby Task Force / MicroMappers (the sector's remote-volunteer backbone):** built *entirely* around digital volunteers who need "no prior experience... just a big heart and access to the Internet" and who "participate entirely remotely without being physically present in disaster areas." Trust is established by **content consensus, not presence or identity**: on MicroMappers, "three volunteers must agree on the classification of tweets or images before the system accepts them." → **Direct confirmation** that a serious, UN-OCHA-activated crisis-response architecture treats remote/digital contribution as a first-class category with its *own* verification model that has nothing to do with physical location. This is the strongest single piece of evidence for the role/action-scoping refinement. (iRevolutions/Meier; Fast Company; UN Chronicle; Standby Task Force)
- **Ushahidi (Kenya 2008 → Haiti 2010):** accepted anonymous reports at volume and solved trust by **corroboration, not identity** — anonymous reports cross-checked against other reports from the same area and against media; credible-but-unverified items published *labelled* unverified. → Reinforces the original review's core finding and the R1 point that geo/identity is *not* the trust mechanism. Trust follows the *report's* corroboration, which is congruent with scoping checks to the submission, not the submitter. (InfoQ; Poynter)
- **Translators Without Borders / CLEAR Global (100,000+ volunteers):** the exact "remote text-based helper" population the principal cited as the reason to reject coordinator-vouched registration. Confirmed: you can volunteer if "fluent in at least one language other than your native language," "don't need formal translation qualifications," and **self-register via an application form with no local coordinator vouching**. Notably, TWB is *"moving away from the verified/non-verified distinction to assign work... most projects now claimable by anyone regardless of status."* → Two findings: (a) the remote-self-registering translator is a **real, large** population, not hypothetical; (b) the sector trend is *away* from hard verification gates for this cohort, toward reputation-enrichment — which argues against making face-dedup a hard blocker for remote registration. (CLEAR Global; TWB)
- **World ID / Worldcoin (the closest real "biometric uniqueness / proof-of-personhood" system):** wants each human to "sign up exactly once." Critically for *this* design: it does **not** keep the check on-device. Uniqueness "is enforced by storing biometric information... in a database on a central server"; on new signup, the sample "is sent to this server which checks whether it is close to any other sample in the database" (recent work scales this via MPC across millions of iris codes). It stores *hashes*, not raw scans — but the comparison is inherently **central**, because catching "same person, second device" requires comparing against *everyone else's* enrollment, which a purely local cache by definition cannot see. → **This is the load-bearing comparable for Q3.** The only production system built to do what R1's dedup *aspires* to (catch a person re-enrolling) concluded it needs a shared surface and went server-side — and still drew heavy criticism for centralized biometric collection. It confirms the design's own stated limitation ("catches same-phone, NOT same-person-on-two-phones") is not an implementation shortcut but a **structural ceiling** of the local-only approach. (arXiv 2405.04463; Worldcoin analyses)
- **On-device face *verification* vendors (Sensory, Microblink, Neurotechnology, Identy):** production 1:1 on-device face auth where "all processing and matching happen locally... nothing uploaded, stored, or sent to the cloud" exists and is marketed for GDPR/CCPA compliance. → Confirms the *technical feasibility* of "never transmitted" client-side face processing. **But** these are 1:1 verification (does this face match *this device's* enrolled owner), not 1:N population dedup — which is the same 1:1-vs-1:N gap above. Feasible for what R1 actually does (same-device compare); not evidence that local dedup solves cross-device Sybil. (Sensory; Microblink; Neurotechnology)
- **DiffUMI / IdDecoder / Deep Face Decoder (face-embedding inversion research, 2023–2025):** the direct answer to Q1 — see Ground-Truth Data. Multiple independent groups reconstruct recognizable faces from embeddings alone. (arXiv 2504.18015; ACM CODASPY 2023; ScienceDirect 2024)
- **Cancelable-biometric / revocable-template literature (ISO/IEC 24745):** the *mitigation* comparable — the standard requires irreversibility + revocability + unlinkability, and recent work ("Deep CNN Face Matchers Inherently Support Revocable Biometric Templates") shows deep face embeddings *can* be transformed so a leaked template is both non-invertible and re-issuable. → This is the concrete engineering answer to "how do we make the local cache safe": store a **salted, per-install, cancelable transform** of the embedding, not the raw embedding. It does not exist in the current R1 spec and should be a named condition. (arXiv 2506.18731; PMC chaotic-system scheme; Inverse Biometrics survey arXiv 2401.02861)

## Ground-Truth Data

- **Face reconstruction from embedding-only — success rate:** DiffUMI (2025) reconstructs a face from *only* the embedding with **Type-II accuracy 74.51%–94.72%** across models/datasets (94.72% on LFW + ArcFace), where "Type-II accuracy" means a **separate face-recognition system confirms the reconstruction as the same identity**. It is black-box-compatible (needs only embedding query access) and **transfers even to explicitly privacy-preserving embedding models (DCTDP, PartialFace): 84.42%–92.87%.** Independently, IdDecoder (ACM CODASPY 2023) and DiffMI/DiffUMI report ~90%+ black-box reconstruction. → **Bottom line for Q1: a raw face embedding is ~75–95% invertible into a re-identifiable face.** "We store the embedding, not the photo" reduces raw-image exposure but is *not* a strong anonymization claim. (arXiv 2504.18015; arXiv/ACM IdDecoder)
- **Regulatory status of a face template (independent of reversibility):** Under GDPR, a facial template created "for the purpose of identifying or authenticating a person" is **biometric special-category data (Art. 9)**, and — per an EDPS decision and multiple analyses — **"pseudonymisation does not remove biometric status"** and "irreversible, unlinkable and revocable [identifiers] are still not anonymized." Even salted hashes "do not make it impossible for templates to be reversed." → For HOS this means: whatever sits in that local cache is legally/ethically **biometric data**, and the "it's just a number" framing does not exempt it. It cannot be rotated if the person's face is compromised (unlike a device key). (GDPR-Advisor; Biometric Update; CaraComp/EDPS; Mobbeel)
- **IndexedDB persistence reality (the "never transmitted" substrate):** IndexedDB is **best-effort by default** — "without StorageManager it's a 'best-effort' database that can be erased in situations of low disk space," evicted **LRU** under pressure, and **cleared when the user clears browsing data**. `navigator.storage.persist()` upgrades it but "may prompt the end user for permission." When an origin is evicted, **all** its data goes at once. → Two consequences: (a) the dedup cache is **not durable** — a user who clears browsing data, or whose phone hits low disk, silently loses the dedup memory and can re-register on the same device (the anti-Sybil property degrades to near-zero for a motivated actor: clear-storage-and-retry). (b) MDN/community sources **do not confirm** whether IndexedDB is swept into OS backup; this is browser/OS-version-dependent and matches the interim note's flagged OS-backup risk — it must be tested, not assumed. (MDN Storage quotas & eviction; Dexie StorageManager docs; dev.to)
- **Remote-mock / spoofing baseline (carried from original review, still governing):** browser geolocation cannot detect mock location ("no way to detect a mock location in a browser" — KoboToolbox/ODK maintainers), so any geo signal is advisory only — which R1 already accepts. This *strengthens* the case for action-scoping: if geo can't be trusted as a control anyway, gating it on a self-declared "role" buys nothing over gating it on whether the *submission even carries a location claim*. (KoboToolbox/ODK forums — see original review)
- **Remote-volunteer population size (sanity check on "is this a real cohort"):** TWB alone reports **100,000+** volunteers, self-registering, remote. The DHN federates many such volunteer-and-technical communities. → The remote/digital contributor is numerically a *major* class, not an edge case bolted on to justify an exemption. (CLEAR Global; DHN/UN Chronicle)

## Assumption Tests

| Assumption (from R1 design) | Verdict | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| "We store the embedding, not the photo" is a meaningful privacy mitigation | **PARTIALLY CONTRADICTED** | A *raw* embedding is 75–95% invertible into a re-identifiable face (DiffUMI Type-II 74.51–94.72%, black-box, transfers to privacy-preserving models). Regulators treat the template as biometric special-category data regardless. It *is* better than storing the photo, but far weaker than "just numbers." Only a **cancelable/salted transform** (ISO 24745) makes it a strong claim — and R1 does not specify one. |
| On-device-only face processing that never leaves the device is technically real/feasible | **SUPPORTED** | Multiple vendors ship on-device 1:1 face verification with nothing uploaded (Sensory, Microblink, Neurotechnology). "Never transmitted" is achievable; it must still be *enforced and tested* (network audit), per the original D4/honesty lesson. |
| A local-only cache can catch a person re-registering | **CONTRADICTED (for cross-device); SUPPORTED (same-device only)** | Catching "same person, new device" requires comparing against *others'* enrollments — inherently a shared surface. World ID, the flagship uniqueness system, went **server-side (central/MPC)** for exactly this reason. Local cache can only see its own device. R1 already concedes this; the evidence confirms it is a structural ceiling, not a gap to close later. |
| The local dedup cache is a reliable anti-Sybil control | **CONTRADICTED (weak)** | IndexedDB is best-effort, LRU-evictable, and **user-clearable** ("clear browsing data" wipes it). A motivated re-registrant clears storage and retries. This is a *speed-bump against accidental/casual* duplicate registration on one device, not a control against a deliberate Sybil actor. Honest framing required. |
| Remote/text-based volunteers (translators, moderators) are a real cohort that must self-register with no local voucher | **SUPPORTED (strongly)** | TWB: 100k+ remote volunteers, self-register, no coordinator vouching, sector *moving away* from verified/unverified gating. DHN/SBTF/MicroMappers built on remote-only digital volunteers. This is the dominant pattern, not a corner case. |
| Crisis-response platforms distinguish remote/digital from field/physical contributors in their trust model | **SUPPORTED** | DHN/SBTF/MicroMappers explicitly separate remote digital tasking (verified by content-consensus) from field presence; Ushahidi trusts by corroboration not identity. The distinction R1 assumes is real and load-bearing in prior art. |
| Geolocation proximity should be a property of "roles" (coordinators/translators exempt) | **RESHAPE → prefer action-scoping** | The prior art trusts the *submission* (corroboration / content-consensus), and geo can't be verified in-browser anyway. Scoping the check to "does this specific submission assert a physical location?" is simpler, matches the sector pattern, needs no self-declared-role trust, and handles the sometimes-remote-sometimes-field volunteer. Supports the interim note's Occam's-razor reshape. |
| OS backup won't leak the local embedding | **UNKNOWN** | Neither MDN nor community sources confirm IndexedDB is excluded from OS/cloud backup; it is browser/OS-version-dependent. Must be tested per target platform or the residual exposure documented, per the interim note. |

## Unknown Unknowns (where R1 — and I — are guessing)

- **Whether a cancelable transform survives the "same-device compare" use case.** The fix for invertibility (salted/cancelable embedding) is well-established for *verification*, but R1 needs to *compare new-registration embedding against previously-cached ones on the same device*. Whether a per-install-salted transform still supports that same-device 1:N compare with acceptable false-match/false-non-match rates on a WASM/TF.js-class in-browser model is not something I can confirm from the literature — it needs a prototype measurement. I flag this as the **thing to prototype first** on the dedup lane: does cancelable-transform + in-browser compare actually work, or does hardening the cache break the very comparison it exists for?
- **Real false-match rate of an in-browser (WASM/TF.js) embedding model on low-end Venezuelan Android + bad cameras.** Same gap the original review flagged for the face lane generally: every accuracy figure here is imported from other populations/hardware. A registration-time dedup that false-*matches* two different volunteers would **wrongly block a legitimate helper from registering** — and does so silently, on-device, with no coordinator to appeal to (the whole point of the remote-self-register cohort). Unmeasured.
- **The Sybil threat model R1 is actually defending against.** If the realistic adversary is a *state actor seeding fake volunteers*, local-cache dedup is trivially defeated (new device, or clear storage). If the realistic concern is merely *accidental duplicate accounts / one person double-registering by mistake*, local dedup is adequate and proportionate. R1 doesn't state which — and the honest value of the feature depends entirely on that. My read: it's worth building **only** as accidental-duplicate hygiene, and must be *marketed internally as such*, never as anti-Sybil, or it repeats the "claimed a capability we don't have" honesty failure the original review drew from D4.
- **Shared-device embedding accumulation (carried, still open).** The interim note's shared-shelter-device scenario is unresolved: do multiple volunteers' embeddings coexist on one device (raising per-seizure blast radius), and is the cache wiped per shift? Needs an explicit answer before ship.
- **Duress / seizure (carried from original).** Unchanged and unsolved by R1: nothing here protects a volunteer whose device is seized and who is compelled to unlock. The local embedding cache is one more biometric artifact on a seized device — argues for aggressive minimization and a wipe/panic path regardless of the dedup decision.

## Confidence Score

**0.7**

Why this high: the two questions this round existed to settle are now **triangulated across independent, authoritative sources**, and both came back clearly.
- Q1 (embedding invertibility): multiple independent 2023–2025 groups (DiffUMI, IdDecoder, Deep Face Decoder) converge on 75–95% embedding-only reconstruction with cross-model transfer, *plus* independent regulatory sources (EDPS/GDPR) treating templates as biometric data regardless of pseudonymization. This is not a lone paper.
- Q2 (remote/field distinction): converges across DHN/SBTF/MicroMappers primary descriptions, Ushahidi's documented corroboration model, and TWB's stated 100k-remote-volunteer self-registration practice. Robust.
The recommendation direction (RESHAPE: on-device is a real improvement; treat the local cache as invertible biometric data and harden it; scope geo to the action not the role; frame dedup as accidental-duplicate hygiene not anti-Sybil) is well-supported.

Why not higher: (1) Q3 is genuinely **mixed, not clean** — I can confirm on-device 1:1 verification is production-real and that 1:N uniqueness went server-side in the one flagship case, but I found **no** production example of purely-local, never-transmitted *uniqueness dedup* to point to as a positive precedent, so I'm reasoning from the *absence* plus first principles rather than a direct comparable. (2) The two most decision-relevant *magnitudes* — the false-match rate of an in-browser model on HOS's actual devices, and whether a cancelable transform preserves the same-device compare — are **unmeasured** and only a prototype settles them. (3) The IndexedDB-vs-OS-backup question I could only establish as "browser/OS-dependent, unconfirmed," not resolve. None of these change the direction; they cap certainty on the details, and this round was explicitly about not overstating what hasn't been tested.
